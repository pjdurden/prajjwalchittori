Credentials Are a Receipt, Not the Meal

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

A credential is proof that you paid. That’s all it is. A degree proves you paid four years and some tuition. A certification proves you paid an afternoon and an exam fee. None of it proves you can actually do the thing — it proves you stood in the right line.

We confuse the receipt for the meal constantly. I’ve watched companies hire the receipt and then act surprised when no food arrives. I’ve done it myself, in reverse — collected receipts I thought I needed, only to find that the work didn’t care.

The reason this matters isn’t fairness. It’s that credentials are a lagging indicator and thoughts are a leading one. By the time you’ve earned a credential, the thing it certifies is already in the past. But a thought — a real one, the kind that reorganizes how you see a problem — is about what you can do next. If you had to bet on a person, you’d want the leading indicator.

Paul Graham noticed years ago that the most credentialed people are often the most cautious, because they have the most to protect. The receipt becomes a thing you defend instead of a thing you spend. That’s the trap. A credential should be ammunition. The moment it becomes armor, you’ve stopped moving.

I’m not anti-credential. I have the degree, the Codeforces rating, the resume. I’m anti-confusing them for the work. The rating got me in the room. It has never once written a line of code for me. The degree opened a door. It has never shipped a product.

Here is the test I use now. If everything I’d been issued — every certificate, title, and badge — vanished overnight, what would I still be able to make by Friday? That number, the make-by-Friday number, is the only honest measure of what you’re worth. Everything else is just receipts.

Keep them. File them. But don’t eat them.
