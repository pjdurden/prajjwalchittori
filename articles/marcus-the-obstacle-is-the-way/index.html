The Bug Is The Work

Prajjwal Chittori · December 2024

There’s a line in Marcus that gets quoted to death, usually badly: “The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.” People turn it into a motivational poster, which is a shame, because the actual idea is more precise and more useful than the poster suggests.

Marcus isn’t saying obstacles are secretly good for you in some cosmic, character-building sense. He’s saying something more like an engineer would say: the obstacle is the material. A fire takes whatever you throw on it and turns it into more fire. It doesn’t get derailed by the log; the log becomes fuel. He wanted a mind that worked the same way — that didn’t treat interruption as separate from the task, but as part of the task’s raw material.

I came to believe this most concretely through bugs. Early on, I treated bugs as interruptions to “the real work.” The real work was building the feature; the bug was a tax on it, a thing standing between me and progress. Then it slowly dawned on me that the bug was the feature. The hard, weird, won’t-reproduce defect is where the actual engineering happens — where you learn how the system really behaves versus how you imagined it behaved. The clean path teaches you nothing. The obstacle is where the information is.

Once you see it this way, your whole relationship to friction changes. The annoying customer isn’t blocking the product; the annoying customer is telling you what the product is missing. The competitor who undercut you isn’t an injustice; they’re a free, brutal audit of where your moat is thin. The thing that stands in the way is rarely just an obstacle. It’s usually the most honest piece of feedback you’re going to get, delivered in the one form you can’t ignore.

This is also why I distrust the dream of the frictionless path — the perfect launch, the runway with no turbulence, the build with no surprises. Not because it’s unrealistic, though it is. Because a frictionless path doesn’t teach you anything, and a thing built without resistance tends to fall apart the first time it meets any. The companies that last got forged against their obstacles. The ones that died usually died in the one direction where they’d never met any.

The discipline Marcus is pointing at is subtle. It’s not enthusiasm for hardship — that’s just a different kind of delusion. It’s the refusal to let the obstacle change the channel. You wanted to make progress; the bug appeared; fine, the bug is now how you make progress. You don’t stop, you don’t sulk, you don’t wait for the path to clear. You feed the log to the fire and keep being fire.

So when something blocks me now, I’ve trained a small reflex before the frustration lands: this thing is in my way, which means this thing is the way. What is it trying to tell me. What does fixing it teach me that the smooth path never would.

The bug isn’t between you and the work. The bug is the work.
