Taste Is the Last Edge

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

For most of my life, taste sounded like a soft word — the thing people invoke when they can’t measure something. Engineers are suspicious of it because it doesn’t show up in a benchmark. I’ve come to think taste is the hardest, most valuable, and most underpriced skill there is, and the age we’re walking into is about to prove it.

Start with a definition, because the word is slippery. Taste is the ability to tell, fast and without a rubric, which of two things is better. Which design is cleaner. Which idea is worth pursuing. Which sentence lands and which is filler. Which feature matters and which is bloat dressed as progress. It’s judgment that runs faster than analysis — you see it before you can fully explain it, and then good taste is also being right when you finally do explain it.

Here’s why it’s about to be the only edge that matters. When execution was expensive, taste was a luxury. It didn’t matter much that you knew which thing to build if building anything took a year, because the cost of building dominated everything. You could be mediocre at choosing and still win on grind. But as the agents drive the cost of execution toward zero, the bottleneck moves entirely to choosing. When you can build anything, the only question left is what’s worth building — and that question has no benchmark, no rubric, no agent. That question is pure taste. The constraint isn’t capability anymore. It’s discernment.

The uncomfortable truth is that taste can’t be downloaded. You can’t read a book and have it. It only comes from one place: an enormous volume of attempts, each one judged against reality, the feedback slowly recalibrating your internal sense of better and worse. I built taste in distributed systems by being wrong about architectures and watching them fall over in production. I built taste in short-form by posting hundreds of times and watching most of it die and a few things spread. There was no shortcut through either. The reps were the curriculum. You buy taste in time and embarrassment, and there’s no other currency it accepts.

Which is exactly why it’s such a durable edge. Anything cheap to acquire is no edge at all — if you can buy it in a weekend, so can everyone. Taste is expensive in the one way that can’t be arbitraged away: it costs years and a thousand judged attempts, and there’s no API for it and there won’t be. The agents can execute any taste you hand them flawlessly. They cannot supply the taste. They’ll happily build the wrong thing with perfect craft, forever, until a human with judgment tells them which thing was worth building.

So I’ve stopped treating taste as the soft afterthought and started treating it as the actual asset — the thing worth the most deliberate practice. Every time I choose, I’m training it. Every time I default to a rubric instead of developing the judgment to not need one, I’m letting it atrophy.

In a world where everyone can build anything, the person who knows what’s worth building wins. That person isn’t the best engineer. They have the best taste. Go get reps.
