Freedom Over Comfort

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Dostoevsky stages a confrontation in The Brothers Karamazov that I’d put on the wall of every company. A returned Christ is arrested by the Grand Inquisitor, an old cardinal, who explains, almost tenderly, why he must be burned. The charge: you gave men freedom, and freedom is unbearable, and we — the Church, the system, the managers of mankind — have spent centuries quietly taking it back, because what people actually want is not freedom but to be relieved of it. Bread, miracle, authority. Someone to decide for them. We took your terrible gift and replaced it with comfort, and the people thanked us.

The Inquisitor is not a villain in the cartoon sense. That’s what makes the passage devastating. He’s right about people, mostly. Most of us, given the choice between freedom and comfort, choose comfort, and are grateful to whoever takes the freedom off our hands. The Inquisitor is the patron saint of every system that offers you security in exchange for your capacity to choose — and there are more of those systems now than ever, each one kinder and more frictionless than the last.

Because this is the exact bargain the modern world runs, and the AI age is its purest form yet. Don’t decide — we’ll recommend. Don’t navigate — we’ll route you. Don’t struggle with the blank page — here’s a draft. Don’t carry the weight of choosing; we’ve optimized the choice away. When AI arrived, it offered convenience and we offered our lives, and convenience is just the Inquisitor’s bread in a sleeker package. Each handoff is reasonable. Each one is comfortable. And the sum of them is a person who has been gently, gratefully relieved of the freedom that made him a person.

I want to be careful, because comfort isn’t evil and struggle isn’t holy. Suffering for its own sake is a different mistake. The point isn’t to refuse every convenience like a martyr. The point is to notice what you’re trading in each transaction, because the trades are invisible one at a time and total when you add them up. The Inquisitor never takes your freedom in one grab. He takes it in a thousand small reliefs, each of which you welcomed.

The builder’s whole stance is a quiet refusal of this bargain. To build is to insist on carrying the freedom that the world keeps offering to carry for you — to face the blank page when there’s a draft on tap, to make the decision when there’s a system happy to make it, to take responsibility when the comfortable move is to let it diffuse into the process. It’s harder. It is meant to be harder. The difficulty is not a bug in freedom; the difficulty is the freedom. A choice with no weight wasn’t yours.

Christ, in the story, doesn’t argue with the Inquisitor. He listens to the entire indictment and then kisses the old man on his bloodless lips and walks out into the night, leaving him free — even the freedom to be wrong, even the freedom to keep choosing comfort. That’s the whole answer. Freedom can’t be defended by argument. It can only be taken, by someone willing to bear it.

The world will keep offering to carry your freedom for you, more sweetly every year. Keep some of it. Carry the weight on purpose. The weight is the proof you’re still the one choosing.
