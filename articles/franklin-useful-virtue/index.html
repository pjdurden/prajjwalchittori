Franklin Only Cared About Useful Virtue

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2022

Benjamin Franklin had a test for ideas, and it was almost rude in its simplicity: does it work? He didn’t much care whether a belief was elegant or orthodox or pleasing to authority. He cared whether it produced results in the world. He praised religion not for being true but for being useful — for making people behave better. He chased virtues not because heaven demanded them but because they got him further in this life. He was, before the word existed, a pragmatist, and I think it’s the most underrated trait a builder can have.

Most people are not actually pragmatists, even the ones who claim to be. They’re loyal to the elegant. They want the architecture that’s beautiful, the idea that’s clever, the approach that signals taste — and they’ll quietly prefer it even when a uglier thing would work better. Franklin would have found this absurd. To him a thing’s virtue was its usefulness. The clever solution that doesn’t ship is less virtuous than the boring one that does, because virtue, for Franklin, lived in the result and nowhere else.

This sounds obvious until you watch how hard people fight it. We are deeply attached to being right in the abstract. The engineer who insists on the theoretically superior approach while the deadline burns. The founder in love with the elegant business model that no customer actually wants. The whole genre of being correct in a way that produces nothing. Franklin’s pragmatism is a constant cold shower on this: I don’t care that it’s elegant, I care that it works, and if the ugly thing works and the elegant thing doesn’t, the ugly thing is better, full stop, and your discomfort about that is vanity.

The deists shared this posture toward belief itself, which is what makes Franklin’s version coherent rather than just cynical. They asked of a doctrine not “is this the inherited truth” but “does this hold up to reason and produce good results.” They cut miracles not out of spite but because miracles didn’t do anything you could verify or build on. It’s the same test Franklin ran on virtues, on inventions, on institutions: strip away the prestige and the tradition and ask what it actually produces. A belief that changes no behavior and predicts no outcome was, to these men, just decoration.

I’ve made this my own filter and it has saved me from a lot of expensive beauty. When I’m attached to an approach, I now interrogate the attachment: do I want this because it works, or because it’s elegant and choosing it makes me feel like a sophisticated person? Those are completely different reasons, and only the first one is allowed to win. The second reason has cost me weeks I’ll never get back — gold-plating, premature abstraction, the lovely general solution to a problem that needed a crude specific one. Every hour of it was me optimizing for how the work made me feel rather than what the work did.

There’s a humility in pragmatism that the elegance-lovers mistake for crudeness. It takes more ego, not less, to insist on the beautiful approach against the evidence — you’re trusting your taste over reality. Franklin trusted reality over his taste. He’d ship the boring thing, watch it work, and feel no loss, because his pride was located in the outcome and not in the cleverness. That’s the move. Relocate your pride from the elegance of your method to the usefulness of your result, and most of your worst decisions just stop being tempting.

Does it work? That was Franklin’s whole test. Run it on your next clever idea and watch how many of them fail it.
