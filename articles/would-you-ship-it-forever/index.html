Would You Ship It Forever?

Prajjwal Chittori · December 2022

Nietzsche proposes a test, and it’s the most useful decision-making tool I know that nobody uses. Imagine a demon visits you at your loneliest hour and says: this life you’ve lived, down to the smallest detail, you will live again — infinitely, the same, forever. The same days, the same work, the same mistakes, on a loop with no exit. Do you curse the demon, or do you fall to your knees and call it divine?

He calls it eternal recurrence. It’s usually taught as cosmology, like he’s making a claim about physics. He isn’t, or it doesn’t matter if he is. It’s a test. The question is: would you want it again? And the genius is that it strips out the one thing that corrupts every decision — the future payoff.

Almost everyone justifies their present by a future. I’m grinding now so that later I’ll arrive. I’m enduring this job so that the exit comes. The whole structure is a loan against a tomorrow that may never pay out, and worse, that converts the present into mere means — a toll you pay to reach a destination. The recurrence test deletes the destination. It says: there is no later. This day repeats forever. Now choose.

Run it on your work and it sorts everything brutally fast. The project you’re doing only for the outcome — the exit, the title, the moment it’s finally over — fails instantly. You would not want that process again. You want the prize, not the days, and a life is made of days, not prizes. But the project where the doing itself is something you’d accept on infinite loop? The problem that’s a punch to the face and you lean into the punch? That one passes. That’s a thing built on the right footing.

I’ve used this on ventures without naming it. The ones I abandoned — some of them, honestly, I never wanted the daily reality of; I wanted the having-built-it. The recurrence test would have flagged them on day one. Would I want to live these specific hours again, forever, regardless of where they lead? For some, no. Knowing that earlier would have saved me months.

This is also the cleanest antidote to a culture that’s all delayed gratification. Delayed gratification is fine until you notice you’ve delayed the entire thing — that you’ve built a life of means with the end always one more year out, a carrot on a stick you’re carrying yourself. Recurrence asks the heretical question: what if the means is all there is? What if this is the life and not the rehearsal? Then a day you’d only tolerate for its payoff is a day you’ve thrown away, because the payoff is a story and the day was real.

The deepest version of the test isn’t about choosing pleasant days. Sisyphus would pass it; his days are hard. It’s about owning your days so completely that you’d take them again with the difficulty included. That’s not contentment. It’s the opposite of regret — a life arranged so that nothing in it is merely survived.

So before the next thing, ask the demon’s question. Not will this pay off. Ask: if I had to do exactly these days forever, would I bless it or curse it?

Build only what you’d ship forever.
