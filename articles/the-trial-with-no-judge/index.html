The Trial With No Judge

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Josef K. is arrested one morning and never told the charge. He spends the whole book trying to find the court, the document, the official who can explain what he did. There is no such official. The horror of The Trial isn’t that the verdict is unfair. It’s that there is no verdict, only the procedure, going on forever.

Most ambitious people are living in that book and don’t know it.

You feel arrested. There’s a charge you can’t name — you’re behind, you’re not enough, you’re wasting it. So you go looking for the court. You think a title will be the court. Then a funding round. Then a follower count, an exit, a number. You present yourself for judgment and the clerk takes your papers and tells you to wait in another room. The judgment never comes. The number arrives and the feeling doesn’t lift, because the feeling was never about the number. There was never a judge. You built the courtroom yourself and then spent your life trying to win a case in it.

I know this courtroom well. When I have a job I feel I’m wasting my potential. When I’m between things I feel I’m not grinding hard enough. Notice that both states are guilty. There is no configuration of facts that produces innocence, which is the tell. A trial you cannot win by changing the facts is not a trial about the facts.

Kafka’s mistake — and he knew it was a mistake, that’s why he wrote it down — was to keep believing in the court. Josef K. could have walked out. He could have said: I do not recognize this proceeding. There is no charge because there is no judge, and I will not arrange my one life around an acquittal that no one is empowered to grant.

That’s the move. Not to win the case but to deny the jurisdiction.

Practically this looks boring. It looks like deciding, in advance and in writing, what enough is for a given thing — and then honoring it when you hit it, instead of letting the goalpost slide the instant you arrive. The ship date. The revenue line. The skill acquired. You name the verdict yourself, out loud, because no external office is going to hand it to you. Credentials don’t give me value; my thoughts do — and the same is true of acquittals. If I won’t grant my own, no court above me will.

This isn’t contentment. I’m not telling you to want less. I want enormous things. But there’s a difference between hunger and a haunting. Hunger drives you toward something. The haunting drives you in a circle through endless corridors, presenting your papers to clerks who cannot read them, in a building with no exit because you keep declining to find the door.

Kafka died with the book unfinished, which is the most honest possible ending. The trial doesn’t conclude. It only stops when you stop showing up to it.

Stop showing up. Go build the thing for its own sake. There is no judge. There was never going to be. That’s not the bad news — that’s the release.
