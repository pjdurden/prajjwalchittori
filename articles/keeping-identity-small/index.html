Keep Your Identity Small, Especially as a Builder

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Paul Graham has a short essay called Keep Your Identity Small. The argument is that the moment a belief becomes part of your identity, you stop being able to think clearly about it — you defend it like territory instead of evaluating it like a claim. He uses it to explain why religion and politics make people stupid in conversation. I want to apply it somewhere he didn’t: to building, where I think it’s the difference between people who keep getting better and people who get stuck.

The trap is subtle because it looks like commitment. You build a thing, it does okay, and slowly the thing fuses to your sense of self. You stop being a person who made a Rust backend and become “a Rust person.” You stop being someone who tried a particular business model and become a believer in it. Now any evidence against the choice isn’t data — it’s an attack on you. You’ll defend a dying approach long past the point of sense, not because you’re stupid, but because killing it now feels like killing a piece of yourself.

I’ve watched this end careers quietly. The engineer who became their favorite framework and couldn’t see the world had moved. The founder who became their original idea and kept it on life support for years because pivoting felt like admitting they were wrong, rather than just the idea. Their identity got large and heavy, and a large identity can’t turn. It has too much surface area committed to being a particular thing, so every change of direction tears something.

The builders who compound are the ones who hold everything loosely except the work itself. They’re not “a crypto person” or “an AI person” — they’re a person who builds, currently pointed at crypto, ready to repoint the instant the leverage moves. The tools, the stack, the domain, even the specific company — all of it is held at arm’s length, swappable, never fused to the self. I’ve tried hard to keep my identity to one line: I build things, and I try to be right about what’s worth building. Everything past that is a temporary position I can abandon the moment it stops paying, without it costing me anything that feels like me.

There’s a cleaner way to say it. Make your identity a verb, not a noun. A noun — “I am a backend engineer,” “I am a Solidity dev” — is a fixed thing the world can make obsolete; the agents are deprecating nouns by the dozen right now. A verb — “I build,” “I learn,” “I ship” — survives any specific skill becoming worthless, because it was never attached to the skill in the first place. When the terrain shifts, the noun-people get stranded defending their territory and the verb-people just start building the next thing.

This connects to why the founders I admire were deists rather than dogmatists. They held beliefs they could update — propositions they’d revise on evidence — instead of identities they had to defend to the death. Jefferson could cut his own holy book apart precisely because his identity wasn’t fused to the parts he removed.

Keep your identity small. Become a verb. Then nothing the world deprecates can take you with it.
