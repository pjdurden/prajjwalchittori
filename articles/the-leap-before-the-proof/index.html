The Leap Before the Proof

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2021

Kierkegaard noticed something uncomfortable about big decisions: the evidence you’d need to make them well is only available after you’ve made them. You want to know if the marriage will work before you marry. You want to know the company will succeed before you quit to start it. But the knowledge lives on the far side of the commitment. You have to jump first and learn the landing on the way down.

He called the jump a leap of faith. People file that under religion and stop reading. That’s a mistake, because the structure is everywhere, and nowhere more than in building.

No founding decision ever has enough information in it. That’s not a flaw in your research; it’s the nature of the thing. If the case were airtight, it wouldn’t require you — anyone could read the proof and act on it, and the opportunity would already be gone. The decisions that matter are precisely the ones where reason takes you to the edge of the cliff, hands you a partial map, and goes quiet. Reason gets you to the edge. It cannot take the step. The step is a different faculty.

Kierkegaard’s villain isn’t the doubter. It’s the person who waits at the edge forever, gathering more information, because gathering feels like progress and never costs anything. He’s the one who reads every book on swimming and calls it training. I know this person intimately; I’ve been him. One more month of validation. One more competitor teardown. One more reason it might not work. The research is real. It’s also, past a certain point, a sophisticated way of not jumping — anguish wearing the costume of diligence.

Here’s the part that took me years to feel. The leap isn’t the absence of thought. It comes after you’ve thought as far as thought goes. You do the work, you build the case, you push reason to its limit — and then you notice the limit, and you notice that the remaining gap cannot be closed by more thinking, only by acting into it. The leap is what you do at the place where analysis runs out. Refusing to leap there isn’t caution. It’s just choosing not to live the part of your life that can’t be de-risked in advance.

And nearly everything worth doing is in that part. The first line of code on a thing no one asked for. The message you send to the person way above your level. The bet on a technology before the market agrees it’s a thing. Each is a small leap, taken on incomplete proof, into a result you can only read after you commit. CF Expert, eight thousand problems — none of that was a guarantee when I started; it was a leap that I’d become someone capable of solving the next one, made daily, before the proof.

Kierkegaard’s deepest move is that the leap is repeated. It’s not one heroic jump and then safety. You wake up the next morning and the ground is unproven again and you choose again. Faith isn’t a state you arrive at. It’s a verb you keep conjugating.

So: think to the edge. Honestly, fully, to the actual edge. And then notice that the edge is where the thinking ends and the living begins — and step.
