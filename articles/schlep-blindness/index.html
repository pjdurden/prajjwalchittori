Schlep Blindness and the Jobs Nobody Wants

Prajjwal Chittori · May 2022

Paul Graham has an essay about schlep blindness — the way your brain protects you from even seeing the businesses that involve tedious, unglamorous, frightening work. Stripe was a great idea sitting in plain sight for years because dealing with banks and compliance looked like a nightmare, so nobody’s imagination would walk all the way up to it. The schlep was a moat made of other people’s flinching.

I work in crypto and payments, which is a field built almost entirely out of schleps other people refused to look at. Liquid staking, when I helped build it past a billion in TVL, was not a clever idea anyone was hiding. The idea was obvious. The schlep was everything around it — the validator operations, the slashing edge cases, the reconciliation, the long boring war with everything that can go wrong when real money is on the line and there’s no undo button. That war was the moat. Anyone could draw the diagram. Almost nobody wanted to live inside it for two years.

Here’s what I’ve come to believe about schleps: they are the most reliable signal of opportunity available to a builder, precisely because they repel people automatically. A good idea with no schlep attracts a hundred competitors by lunch. A good idea wrapped in eighteen months of grinding, regulated, unglamorous misery attracts almost no one, which means the few who push through own the whole thing. The suffering is not the price of the opportunity. The suffering is the opportunity, because it’s what keeps the field empty.

The trap is that schlep blindness is invisible from the inside. You don’t experience it as “I am avoiding hard work.” You experience it as “that idea isn’t interesting” or “that market is boring” or “someone smarter would’ve done it already.” Your mind launders the avoidance into a reasoned judgment so you never have to notice you flinched. The defense is almost mechanical: when an idea makes you go ugh, the operational stuff, that ugh is a treasure map. The ugh is pointing at the part of the market where you’d have no competition.

Crypto specifically is rich with this. The interesting-sounding parts — the tokenomics, the protocol design, the part you can tweet about — are crowded with the smartest, most easily-bored people on earth. The unsexy parts — the off-chain reconciliation, the boring rails that make a card actually swipe, the compliance plumbing that makes the magic legal — are nearly deserted. Guess which side actually moves money.

So I’ve trained myself to do something unnatural: when I notice myself dismissing an idea as boring, I stop and ask whether I’m seeing clearly or just protecting myself from the schlep. Half the time it really is boring. The other half, it’s the best opportunity I’ve seen all year, wearing a disguise my own brain put on it.

Run toward the ugh. Nobody else will be there.
