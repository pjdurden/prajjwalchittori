Memento Mori Is A Shipping Strategy

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

The Romans had a tradition that a general riding in triumph through the streets, crowd roaring, had a servant behind him whose only job was to keep whispering: remember you will die. At the peak of the highest day of his life, someone was paid to remind him it ends. Marcus took this private and made it a daily instrument. You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do and say and think.

Most people treat memento mori as a melancholy thing — a reason to be sad, or worse, a reason to “live for the moment” in the cheap sense of buying things and skipping work. That’s a total misreading. For Marcus, the thought of death wasn’t a depressant. It was an editor. It cut through the noise of the urgent-but-meaningless and surfaced the few things that actually mattered, because nothing focuses triage like a deadline you can’t negotiate.

I’ve come to use it almost mechanically, as a shipping strategy. When I can’t decide whether something is worth doing, I ask the memento mori question: if I had a sharply limited amount of time, would this make the cut? Most things don’t. The status meeting doesn’t. The feature nobody asked for doesn’t. The argument I want to win on the internet definitely doesn’t. The thing that survives the question — build the actual product, get the real skill, make the thing that exists — is usually the thing I was avoiding by staying busy with everything else.

Death is also the reason I distrust “someday.” Someday I’ll start the company. Someday I’ll learn the hard thing. Someday I’ll do the work that scares me. Memento mori treats someday as the lie it is, because someday is drawn from an account of time you can’t actually verify the balance of. You might have the decades. You might not. Marcus’s move is to act as though the runway is short — not in panic, but in priority. Ship the thing now, because “later” is a promise written against a fund that could close without notice.

There’s a quieter benefit too, and it’s the one I value most. The awareness of ending takes the venom out of small failures. A bad launch matters enormously if this is forever; it matters very little against the backdrop of the whole short span. The same thought that makes you ship faster also makes you flinch less, because the stakes of any single attempt get correctly tiny next to the fact that you’re going to be doing this for a finite, countable number of years and then not at all.

So I keep the servant on my shoulder. Not as gloom — as a forcing function. Remember you will die. Therefore: stop polishing the thing nobody sees. Stop saving the bold move for a later that isn’t promised. Stop spending your limited, unrefundable attention on what wouldn’t survive the question.

The whisper isn’t there to make you sad. It’s there to make you ship.
