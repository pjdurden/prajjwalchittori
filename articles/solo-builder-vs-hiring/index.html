The Case Against Hiring

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

The default startup story has a moment everyone treats as graduation: you hire. You get headcount, an office, an org chart. The press calls it scaling. I want to make the unfashionable case that for a certain kind of builder, hiring is where the thing you loved quietly dies.

Start with the honest accounting nobody does. The instant you hire someone, your job changes from building to managing, and these are not adjacent skills — they’re different jobs that happen to share a title. Paul Graham nailed the schedules: a maker needs long unbroken blocks; a manager’s day is chopped into meeting-sized pieces. The moment you have reports, you are on the manager’s schedule whether you wanted it or not. The very thing that made you good — those deep hours with your hands directly on the work — is the first casualty of the hire that was supposed to give you leverage.

For most of history this trade was forced. You couldn’t build anything substantial alone, so growth meant people, and people meant managing them, and that was simply the cost of scale. There was no other door. But that constraint is dissolving in real time. A single capable builder with modern tools and a swarm of agents can now produce what used to take a team of ten. The leverage that once required headcount is starting to come from software. Which means the central reason to hire — I physically cannot do enough alone — is becoming false for more and more of the work.

So the question flips from “when do I hire” to “do I need to at all.” And the answer, more often than people admit, is no — not because you can’t find good people, but because every hire imports a tax you stop noticing only because everyone pays it. Coordination overhead. Communication that used to happen instantly inside one head now happening slowly across many. Meetings that exist only to undo the confusion that having multiple people created. Politics, eventually, always. The team is a cost center that hides as a growth engine, and most founders never run the comparison because “scaling” sounds like winning.

I’m not romantic about it. Some things genuinely require many hands, and if you’re building a rocket, build a team and lead it well. But most things are not rockets. Most things are a good product that needs to reach the right people, and that can be done by one person with taste, leverage, and distribution — kept small on purpose. Staying small isn’t a phase you tolerate until you can afford people. It can be the design.

Here’s the part that took me longest to accept about myself: I don’t want to manage. For years I treated that as a flaw, a ceiling on my ambition, something to outgrow. Now I read it as a spec. I want my hands on the work. I want the maker’s schedule. I want to build excellent things and ship them and not run a forty-person org I’d spend my days refereeing.

The agents are about to make the solo path more powerful than the team path for an enormous range of work. The most ambitious move of the next decade might not be building a big company. It might be refusing to.
