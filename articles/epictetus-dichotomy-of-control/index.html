The Only Question That Matters In An Incident

Prajjwal Chittori · February 2022

Epictetus was born a slave. He had almost nothing he could control about his external life — not his body, not his freedom, not his circumstances. So he built an entire philosophy out of the one thing nobody could take from him: what he did with his own mind. His handbook opens with a single distinction that he thought was the whole game. Some things are up to us, and some things are not. Confuse the two and you suffer. Sort them correctly and you’re free.

I think about this every time a launch goes sideways. The instinct in a crisis is to spend your energy on the part you don’t control — the market’s reaction, the competitor’s timing, the customer who’s already furious, the number that already dropped. All of it real, none of it yours. Epictetus would say you’re trying to grab the wrong end of the rope. The price moved; that’s not up to you. The user churned; not up to you. What’s up to you is the next decision, the next message, the next fix. That’s a much smaller surface, which is exactly why it’s the only one worth touching.

Engineers actually have a clean technical version of this. We call it the blast radius. You scope the failure, you find the boundary of what you can affect, and you act inside it. What you don’t do — if you’re any good — is run around the parts of the system you have no access to, yelling at them to behave. The dichotomy of control is the same move applied to a career and a nervous system. Find the boundary of your agency. Pour everything inside it. Spend nothing outside it.

Here’s the part people miss, though. The dichotomy isn’t an excuse to relax. It’s the opposite. Once you’ve correctly identified what’s up to you, you have no excuses left about that part. You can’t blame the market for your shipping speed. You can’t blame the funding climate for whether you wrote the thing well. The doctrine takes away the comfort of external blame and hands you total, uncomfortable responsibility for the small zone that’s actually yours. Most people quietly hate this. They’d rather have a big territory they can complain about than a small one they’re accountable for.

I find it clarifying to the point of being aggressive about it. In any mess, I ask one question: which part of this is up to me? Then I treat everything else as weather. The weather isn’t good or bad; it just is, and arguing with it is the purest form of wasted motion. The deal fell through — weather. Someone copied the idea — weather. The thing I shipped is slow — that’s mine, and I’d better fix it.

You don’t get to choose your circumstances. A slave understood that better than any of us will. You only ever get to choose your response, and that turns out to be enough to build a life on.

Sort the rope. Pull the end that’s yours.
