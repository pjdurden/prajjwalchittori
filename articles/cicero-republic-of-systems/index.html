Build A Republic, Not A Kingdom

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Cicero loved the Roman republic the way some engineers love a good system — not for any one of its rulers, but for its design. Res publica, the public thing, a machine for governing that wasn’t supposed to depend on the genius or virtue of whoever happened to be in charge. He spent his life defending it against men who wanted to make it a kingdom, who wanted the system to route through them personally. He lost that fight, and it cost him his head. But the idea he was defending — that the best structures don’t depend on any single irreplaceable person — outlived Rome, ran straight into Jefferson and Franklin, and built America.

I think about this distinction constantly when I build, because there are two ways to make something work, and they look identical from the outside until they’re tested. You can build a kingdom — a thing that runs because you run it, that holds together because of your personal heroics, your context, your willingness to be paged at 3am, your irreplaceable knowledge in your irreplaceable head. Or you can build a republic — a thing designed to run without you, where the knowledge is in the system, the process survives any one person leaving, and no single human is load-bearing.

The kingdom feels great in the moment. You’re indispensable, you’re the hero, everything routes through you, and that’s intoxicating to the ego. But a kingdom is fragile in exactly the place that matters: it dies when the king does, or burns out, or leaves, or simply can’t scale to the next order of magnitude. Every system that depends on one heroic person is one bad week away from collapse, and the heroism that holds it together is also the thing preventing it from ever becoming bigger than that one person’s arms.

The republic is less flattering and far stronger. When you build so the thing runs without you — documented, automated, legible, owned by a structure rather than a person — you’ve made something that can outlive your attention, survive your absence, and grow past your personal ceiling. This is the whole difference between a founder who builds a company and one who builds an expensive job for himself. The founder builds a republic. The other one crowned himself and called it success.

There’s a personal freedom in this too, which is the part Cicero and the deists understood best. A republic doesn’t need a king reaching in to keep it alive — that was the entire point, a system that runs on its own design rather than on perpetual intervention. When you build your work that way, you free yourself. The thing that doesn’t depend on you is the thing you can finally walk away from, hand off, or build on top of, instead of being chained to forever as its sole functioning organ.

So I try to ask, of anything I build: is this a kingdom or a republic? Does it need me, or does it run? The ego wants the kingdom — wants to be needed, central, irreplaceable. The builder who’s actually free wants the republic — the system so well-made that it no longer requires the maker. Make yourself unnecessary to the thing you made. That’s not the failure of building. It’s the completion of it.

Don’t build something that needs you. Build something that doesn’t.
