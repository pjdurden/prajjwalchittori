The Mean Is Not the Middle

Prajjwal Chittori · December 2023

Most people who quote Aristotle’s golden mean think it means moderation. Be brave but not reckless, generous but not a sucker, find the comfortable middle and live there. This is a misreading, and it’s the kind of misreading that produces mediocre engineers and mediocre companies.

The mean is not the average. Aristotle is explicit: the virtuous point lies between two vices, but it is not equidistant from them, and for some people in some situations it sits much closer to one extreme. Courage is closer to recklessness than to cowardice. The mean is the right amount — and the right amount depends entirely on who you are, what you’re doing, and when. It’s a moving target. That’s the whole point.

I think about this constantly when I build systems. There’s a vice of over-engineering — the abstraction layers nobody asked for, the config flags for cases that will never happen, the premature generality that turns a two-week feature into a quarter. And there’s the opposite vice, the cowboy who ships a money-moving service with no idempotency, no reconciliation, and a prayer. Junior engineers swing wildly between these. Seniors are praised for “finding the balance.” But balance is the wrong word. It implies a fixed midpoint you can memorize.

The truth is harder. The right amount of engineering for a hackathon demo and the right amount for a system that settles real value are not the same amount, and they’re not even on the same scale. A great engineer doesn’t carry one balance point around. They re-derive the mean for every situation. That re-derivation — Aristotle calls it phronesis, practical wisdom — is the actual skill. It can’t be written down as a rule, because any rule precise enough to follow is too rigid to be right twice.

This is why you can’t learn judgment from a checklist, and why the ten-years-experience cliché contains a real thing under the cliché. Phronesis is the residue of having gotten the amount wrong many times and felt the cost. You over-engineered and watched a competitor ship first. You under-engineered and got paged at 3am while money was stuck in a contract. Each failure recalibrates your sense of where the mean sits for you, here, now. You’re not accumulating facts. You’re tuning an instrument.

The trap is that calling it “balance” makes it sound safe and centrist, when the mean is often extreme. For a founder at zero, the virtuous amount of risk looks insane to a salaried person — and that’s correct, because the situations are different and the mean moves. The cautious path isn’t more virtuous. It’s just a different vice wearing a respectable coat.

So when someone tells me to find the balance, I hear someone who has stopped re-deriving and started coasting on an average. The mean is not a place you arrive at. It’s a calculation you redo every single time, with full attention, knowing the answer will be different than last time. Aristotle didn’t promise comfort. He promised that the right amount exists, and that finding it is the work of a life.
