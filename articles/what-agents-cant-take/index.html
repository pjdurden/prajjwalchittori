What the Agents Can't Take

Prajjwal Chittori · January 2021

I’ve said for a while that everything repeatable will eventually be replaced by autonomous agents. People hear it as doom. I mean it as a map.

A map needs an edge — a line where one territory ends and another begins. The agents have a clean one. If a task can be specified, demonstrated a few thousand times, and graded, it falls on their side of the line. That covers more of my job than I’d like to admit. Most of what a backend engineer does on a Tuesday is repeatable: wire the endpoint, write the test, reconcile the ledger, follow the runbook. None of that is safe, and pretending it is just means being surprised later.

So the useful question isn’t what will survive — it’s what’s on my side of the line. And the honest answer is: the parts that can’t be specified, because nobody knows what they should be yet.

An agent can build the thing you describe. It cannot decide the thing is worth building. It can optimize the metric you hand it. It cannot tell you the metric is wrong, that you’re measuring the easy thing because the real thing is hard to see. It will happily execute a plan off a cliff with perfect uptime. Taste, problem-selection, knowing which schlep is worth the suffering and which is a trap — that’s not a skill the agents lack temporarily. It’s a different category of work. It’s the work of saying this, not that, before anyone can prove you right.

Here’s the part people miss. As execution gets cheap, judgment gets expensive. When building the thing costs nothing, the entire value moves to deciding what and why. For most of history those were bundled — you couldn’t think about a bridge without spending years learning to build one, so the builders made the calls. The agents are about to unbundle them. The people who only ever had the building half are in trouble. The people who have the deciding half just got a workforce.

This is good news for a specific kind of person and terrible news for another. If your value was being a reliable executor of known tasks, the floor is rising to meet you. If your value was knowing which task mattered — having a thesis, a sense of taste, the nerve to choose — you’ve been handed leverage no generation before you had. Paine wrote pamphlets one at a time and changed a continent. Imagine what he’d do with a printing press that also wrote.

I don’t fix my anxiety about this by getting faster at the repeatable parts. That’s racing a machine at the one thing it’s built to win. I fix it by spending more of my time on the unspecifiable parts — choosing problems, developing taste, building the judgment that decides where the agents get pointed.

The agents are coming for everything that can be written down. So stop competing to write things down faster. Go be the one who decides what gets written.
