Self-Overcoming, Not Conquest

Prajjwal Chittori · March 2020

Will to power is the most misread idea in philosophy. People hear it and picture a boot on a neck — dominate, win, crush. That reading is so popular it’s basically a cottage industry, and it gets Nietzsche almost exactly backwards. The will to power he actually cared about is aimed inward. It’s the will to overcome yourself.

His own phrase: man is something that shall be overcome. Not your competitor. Not the market. You. The most contempt-worthy thing in Nietzsche is not the weak person you could beat — it’s the last man, the one who has stopped wanting to become anything, who has traded all his hunger for a warm blanket and a little pleasure for the day and asks “what is love? what is creation?” and blinks. The last man is comfortable. That’s the whole horror of him.

I bring this up because the domination reading is a trap that wrecks careers, and the careers it wrecks are usually the talented ones. You start to think strength means making other people smaller. You optimize for looking like the most powerful person in the room. And it works, briefly, and then you notice you’ve stopped growing, because you’ve routed all your energy into beating people who were never the obstacle. The obstacle was always the gap between who you are and who you could be. Other people were a distraction from the only fight that compounds.

Here’s the engineering version, because it’s cleaner. The hardest System Design problem in your life is the one whose constraints are your own habits. Every real DSA problem is a punch to the face — fine — but the deepest one is the standing inability you carry from project to project: the thing you keep avoiding, the muscle you won’t build, the fear you keep routing around. Overcoming a rival gives you a trophy. Overcoming that gives you a new self, and the new self gets to attempt things the old one couldn’t even see. One is addition. The other is a different person.

Nietzsche’s word for the highest type is Übermensch, and the mistranslation industry turned it into a superman flying over lesser men. Read him and you find something quieter and harder: a person who has given himself his own law. Who isn’t running on the morality he inherited or the applause he can extract, but on values he forged and now has to live up to. That’s not domination. That’s the heaviest responsibility there is, because there’s no one above you to blame when you fall short of it.

God is dead, he said, and the line gets quoted like a celebration. It isn’t. It’s a warning. It means the scoreboard came down. No cosmic referee is keeping your stats. Most people, faced with that, reach for a substitute referee — a boss, a follower count, a crowd to dominate — anything to feel ranked. Nietzsche’s dare is to refuse the substitute and rank yourself against yourself. Yesterday’s you versus today’s.

That contest never lets you become the last man. You can win it every single day and the bar just rises, which sounds exhausting and is actually the only freedom on offer. Beat the world and you’ve beaten the world. Beat yourself and you’ve changed what’s possible.

Pick the harder fight. It’s the only one that makes you.
