Why I Read

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

People who know me as a backend engineer who grinds competitive programming are mildly confused that I also read Kafka and Seneca and Paine for pleasure. The assumption is that reading philosophy is a kind of self-improvement chore, or worse, decoration — something you do to seem deep. I read for a more selfish and practical reason than that, and I want to make the practical case, because the soft case bores me too.

Reading is the highest-leverage activity that exists, and the reason is almost embarrassingly simple. A book is a person’s best thinking, distilled over years, that you can absorb in days. Marcus Aurelius spent a lifetime and an empire arriving at his conclusions about control and ego and death. I can have the compressed output of that life in an afternoon. There is no other transaction in the world with that exchange rate. You give hours; you receive years. Nothing else you can do with your time even comes close to that multiple.

But that’s the obvious case, and the obvious case undersells it. The deeper reason I read is that it’s the cheapest way to borrow other operating systems for your mind. Every one of us runs on a default OS — assumptions absorbed from our specific family, country, era, all installed before we were old enough to consent. You can’t see your own defaults, because you’re looking through them, not at them. Reading someone from a different time or culture or temperament is the only way I know to briefly run a different OS and finally notice the shape of your own — to see the water you’ve been swimming in your whole life.

This makes me a better builder in a way that’s direct, not decorative. A product is a theory about how people should do something. To build a good one you have to model minds unlike your own — what they want, fear, and value, which is usually nothing like what you want, fear, and value. Reading widely is the cheapest mind-modeling there is. Every novel is supervised training data for empathy. Every philosopher is a worked example of a different way to weigh what matters. The engineer who only reads engineering can build the thing right but keeps building things people don’t actually want, because they’ve never run anyone else’s OS and so they model everyone as a copy of themselves.

There’s a sharper edge to it in this specific decade. As the agents absorb more of the executable, repeatable work, the human edge collapses onto judgment, taste, and the ability to ask the right question — and those are forged almost entirely by what you’ve read and thought. The agents have read everything, in a sense, but they don’t have a self that the reading reshapes. You do. Reading is how you become the kind of person who knows which question is worth asking — and that, increasingly, is the entire remaining job.

So I don’t read to relax, though it does relax me. I don’t read to seem thoughtful, though I’m sure it sometimes reads that way. I read because it’s the best return on time I’ve ever found, and because in an age where execution is going free, the only durable asset left is a well-furnished mind. Borrow as many minds as you can. It’s the cheapest way to build a better one.
