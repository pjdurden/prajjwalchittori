The Single Individual

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Kierkegaard wanted only one phrase on his gravestone: that individual. He spent his life attacking what he called “the crowd,” and his charge against it was strange and specific. He didn’t say the crowd is usually wrong, though he thought it often was. He said something stronger: the crowd is untruth. Not mistaken — incapable of truth, structurally, by being a crowd at all. Because the crowd’s defining feature is that it dilutes responsibility. In a crowd, no single person has to answer for anything. Everyone did it, so no one did. The numbers are an anesthetic.

This is the most underrated idea for anyone trying to build something real, and it cuts against nearly everything the modern internet trains into you.

We are taught to seek the crowd’s verdict as the measure of worth. Consensus is treated as a proxy for truth — if many people agree, it’s probably right; if a thing has traction, it’s probably good. Kierkegaard’s claim is that this is exactly backwards for the things that matter most. Truth, the kind you have to live, is always encountered by a single individual standing alone, answerable, unable to hide in the count. The moment you let the crowd decide for you, you’ve handed off the one thing that was yours: the responsibility to judge, and to be wrong as yourself rather than safe as part of a mass.

In building, the crowd shows up as the metric. How many liked it, how many agreed, how many are doing the same thing. And the metric is genuinely useful for some questions — distribution, demand, what people will pay for. But it’s lethal for the deepest question, which is should this exist, and do I believe in it. That one can only be answered by the single individual, because the crowd is constitutionally unable to answer it. A crowd can tell you what’s popular. It cannot tell you what’s true, because it has no self to stake on the answer. Outsource your conviction to it and you’ll build only what already has consensus — which, by definition, doesn’t need you.

There’s a courage cost here that Kierkegaard names plainly. To be the single individual is to give up the anesthetic. When you decide alone, you can’t spread the blame if you’re wrong — there’s no crowd to dissolve into, no “everyone thought so.” You’re exposed. That exposure is the price of conviction, and it’s why most people quietly prefer the crowd even while complaining about it: the crowd is wrong sometimes but it never leaves you holding the bag alone.

This is also, I think, the real answer to the age of the algorithmic feed, which is the crowd industrialized — a machine purpose-built to tell you, every second, what the mass approves of. To build anything that isn’t a regression to that mean, you have to be able to stand as the single individual against the loudest, most quantified crowd in human history, and say: I have looked, and I judge differently, and I’ll answer for it myself. Credentials don’t give me value; my thoughts do — and a thought you only hold because the crowd holds it was never yours to begin with.

The crowd is untruth. Not because it’s stupid, but because it lets you off the hook — and being on the hook, alone, answerable, is the only place real conviction has ever come from.

Be that individual. Then build what only you can see.
