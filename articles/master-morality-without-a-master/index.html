Master Morality Without a Master

Prajjwal Chittori · May 2018

Nietzsche draws a distinction that gets him in trouble because people read it as a class system. It isn’t. Master morality and slave morality are not about who has power over whom. They’re about where your values come from — whether you generate them or merely react to someone else’s.

Master morality, in his sense, is values created from a yes. You look at the world, you affirm what you find good — strength, creation, courage, generosity, the high thing — and you call it good because you esteem it, full stop. Slave morality is values created from a no. It starts by looking at the powerful, the admired, the people who have what you don’t, and declaring them evil first — and then defining your own goodness as simply being not-them. The whole value system is downstream of resentment. It needs an enemy to define itself against. Take the enemy away and there’s nothing left, because there was never an affirmation underneath, only the no.

He has a word for the engine of slave morality: ressentiment. Not anger — anger acts and moves on. Ressentiment is the stored, fermenting kind that can’t act, so it turns inward and rewrites the scoreboard. It can’t beat the strong, so it declares strength a sin. It can’t build, so it calls building vulgar. Its entire creativity goes into reasons the grapes were sour.

I bring this into the world of building because tech runs on a quiet ocean of ressentiment and most of us are swimming in it without noticing. That founder only succeeded because of luck. That engineer is just good at self-promotion. The people who win are the people who sold out. Each of these might occasionally be true. But notice the structure: it’s a value system built entirely on a no, on defining your goodness as not-being-the-person-who-won. It’s the slave’s move. It feels like clear-eyed criticism and it’s actually a way of not having to compete — you’ve redefined the game so that not playing is the virtuous position.

The master’s move, in the clean Nietzschean sense, is to skip the enemy entirely. Don’t define yourself against the people who succeeded. Define yourself toward what you actually esteem. Affirm the thing you want to build because it’s good to you, not because it’ll prove someone else wrong. The yes needs no villain. And it’s far more powerful, because resentment runs out the moment the enemy leaves the room, while affirmation generates its own fuel forever.

Now the part that matters most, given how this idea has been abused. Master morality is not domination, and the master is not someone who lords over others. The aristocracy Nietzsche admires is an aristocracy of self-command — the person strong enough to give himself his own values and live up to them, who has no need to push anyone down because his sense of worth doesn’t come from comparison at all. The man who must dominate to feel high is still defining himself against others; he’s a slave wearing a crown. The true master is indifferent to the ranking. He’s busy.

God is dead, and the scoreboard came down with him — which means no one is going to hand you a values system, and the only two ways to make your own are the master’s yes and the slave’s no. The slave’s is easier and it poisons you. The master’s is harder and it’s the only one that builds.

Stop ranking yourself against the people you resent. Affirm what you esteem and go make it.

You don’t need a master to defy. You need values to obey — your own.
