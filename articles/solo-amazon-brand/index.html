₹2 Lakh in Month One: What a Backend Engineer Learned Building a Physical Brand Solo

Prajjwal Chittori · October 2025

I write smart contracts and payments backends for a living. So when I decided to build a consumer cleaning brand — NAMELESS, sold under my company KuruX Enterprises, with a sponge-wipe line called WipeZilla — I assumed the software would be the hard part. It was the easiest part. Here’s what the first month actually taught me, ending with ~₹2 lakh in revenue and a very honest ₹11K in profit.

The software was 5% of the work

Listing pages, a storefront, the website — a weekend. Then reality: manufacturer relations, inventory, compliance, trademarks, barcodes, packaging, returns. As an engineer I’m used to systems where the rules are written down. Physical commerce is a system where the rules are implied, distributed across a dozen people who’ve never met, and discovered only by getting them wrong once. I onboarded a supply chain the way you reverse-engineer an undocumented API: poke, observe, adjust.

Distribution is the entire game

A good product with no distribution is a hobby. About 90% of my early sales came from people who already knew me. That’s a humbling number, and it’s the most important data point of the whole experiment: the first sales measure your network, not your product. The real question a founder has to answer is what happens after your contacts are exhausted — and that’s a distribution problem, not a manufacturing one. Everything I’d label “growth” lives downstream of solving it.

Thin margins change how you think about everything

₹2 lakh in, ₹11K out. Once you’ve felt margins that thin, you stop romanticizing revenue. You start asking the operator’s questions: what’s the contribution margin per unit, what does a return actually cost me, which SKU is subsidizing which. Software people are trained to optimize for scale and ignore unit economics because marginal cost is ~zero. Physical goods beat that instinct out of you in a month, and it makes you a sharper builder everywhere — including in software, where “cheap to serve one more user” quietly hides a lot of waste.

Doing every role is the fastest MBA

For one month I was manufacturing, compliance, marketing, customer service, and logistics. You learn more about a business by being every seat for thirty days than by reading about it for a year. I now understand viscerally why companies are structured the way they are — every org chart is a map of problems someone got tired of personally owning.

Knowing when to hand it off

I built it to a working, growing operation and then handed it to my family to run, rather than letting it die from my own attention drifting elsewhere. That’s its own lesson: a solo builder’s scarcest resource is sustained focus, and the mature move is to recognize what you’re good at (zero-to-one) and route what you’re not (steady operations) to someone who is. Building something is not the same skill as running it. Pretending they’re the same kills more small businesses than competition does.

If you’re an engineer thinking about it

Do it once. Not because it’ll be your fortune, but because nothing else will teach you distribution, margins, and operations at this clip. You’ll come back to your code a better systems thinker — more honest about cost, more respectful of the parts of a system that aren’t written down.
