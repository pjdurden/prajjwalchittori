Spinoza's God Is Just the System Running

Prajjwal Chittori · July 2023

Spinoza got excommunicated for an idea that sounds, to a modern engineer, almost boring. God and Nature are the same thing — Deus sive Natura, God or Nature, pick one, they’re identical. No being outside the universe reaching in to adjust it. The universe and its laws are the divine. Understand the rules and you understand God, because the rules are all that God is.

Amsterdam threw him out for this. Three centuries later it’s the working theology of everyone who’s ever debugged a distributed system at scale, whether they’d say it that way or not.

Here’s the thing I mean. Operate a large system long enough and you stop believing in intervention. The new engineer thinks there’s a hand — a hero, an architect, a heroic on-call who reaches in and fixes things by will. The experienced operator knows there’s no hand. There’s the system and its laws. Throughput is what the laws permit. Latency is what the laws permit. You don’t fix it by reaching in and demanding; you fix it by understanding the rules well enough to change the rules. No miracle layer. Just nature, and how well you grasp it.

This is Spinoza’s discipline, and it’s hard because it kills the comforting fantasy of the savior. His God doesn’t answer prayers, doesn’t make exceptions, doesn’t love you specially, won’t suspend the rules because you’re stressed and it’s Friday. The rules hold. Always. Your only power is understanding them well enough to work with them. He called the highest human state the “intellectual love of God” — decoded, the deep joy of finally understanding how the machine actually works. Anyone who’s truly grasped a gnarly system after weeks of confusion knows that joy. It’s almost religious. Spinoza would say it is religious, and the religion is comprehension.

There’s a freedom hidden in the determinism, the part everyone misses. People hear “the laws always hold, no exceptions” and feel trapped. Spinoza felt the opposite. Freedom was understanding necessity — the more clearly you see why things must be as they are, the less you flail against them and the better you act. The engineer who accepts that CAP is real, that latency is physics, that you can’t have all three, is freer than the one still demanding a miracle. You stop arguing with the universe and start designing within it. That’s not resignation. That’s competence.

I find this the sanest posture toward a hard problem. No hand is coming to save the launch. There’s the system, its real laws, and how well I understand them. The grace available is exactly zero, and that’s fine, because grace was never how anything got built. Comprehension was.

Spinoza’s God is the system running. You worship it by understanding it so completely you can change what it does. Everything else is just praying to a machine that was never listening.
