Spinoza's God Is Just the System Running

Prajjwal Chittori · July 2023

Spinoza got excommunicated for an idea that sounds, to a modern engineer, almost boring. He said God and Nature were the same thing — Deus sive Natura, God or Nature, take your pick, they’re one. There’s no being outside the universe reaching in to adjust it. The universe and its laws are the divine. To understand the rules is to understand God, because the rules are all that God is.

The Amsterdam community threw him out for this. Three centuries later it’s the working theology of everyone who has ever debugged a distributed system at scale, whether they’d put it that way or not.

Here’s what I mean. When you operate a large system long enough, you stop believing in intervention. The new engineer thinks there’s a hand — a hero, an architect, a heroic on-call who can reach in and fix things by will. The experienced operator knows there’s no hand. There’s only the system and its laws. The throughput is what the laws permit. The latency is what the laws permit. You don’t fix the system by reaching in and demanding; you fix it by understanding the rules so deeply that you can change the rules. There is no miracle layer. There’s just nature, and your comprehension of it.

This is Spinoza’s discipline, and it’s a hard one because it removes the comforting fantasy of the savior. Spinoza’s God doesn’t answer prayers, doesn’t make exceptions, doesn’t love you specially, won’t suspend the rules because you’re stressed and it’s Friday. The rules hold. Always. The only power you have is the power of understanding them well enough to work with them. He called the highest human state the “intellectual love of God” — which, decoded, means the deep joy of finally understanding how the machine actually works. Anyone who has ever truly grasped a gnarly system after weeks of confusion knows that joy. It’s almost religious. Spinoza would say it is religious, and that the religion is comprehension.

There’s a freedom hidden in the determinism, which is the part everyone misses. People hear “the laws always hold, no exceptions” and feel trapped. Spinoza felt the opposite. He thought freedom was understanding necessity — that the more clearly you see why things must be as they are, the less you flail against them, and the more effectively you act. The engineer who accepts that CAP is real, that latency is physics, that you cannot have all three — that engineer is freer than the one who keeps demanding a miracle. You stop arguing with the universe and start designing within it. That’s not resignation. That’s competence.

I find this the sanest possible posture toward a hard problem. There is no hand coming to save the launch. There is only the system, its real laws, and how well I understand them. The amount of grace available is exactly zero, and that’s fine, because grace was never how anything got built. Comprehension was.

Spinoza’s God is the system running. You worship it by understanding it so completely that you can change what it does. Everything else is just praying to a machine that was never listening.
