No One Can Actually Insult You

Prajjwal Chittori · February 2025

Epictetus says something that sounds absurd until you’ve sat with it for a while: “If anyone tells you that a certain person speaks ill of you, do not make excuses about what is said of you, but answer: He was ignorant of my other faults, else he would not have mentioned these alone.” The man has been insulted and his response is essentially: he doesn’t even know the half of it. Underneath the joke is the actual doctrine — what wounds you in an insult isn’t the words. It’s your agreement with them. Remove the agreement and the wound has nothing to land on.

His sharper formulation: it’s not the person who reviles you who harms you, but your judgment that you’ve been harmed. The insult is just sound waves and intent until your own mind picks it up, ratifies it, and turns it into damage. Which means the power was never really with the insulter. It was with you, at the moment you decided to be hurt — a decision so fast it doesn’t feel like a decision at all, which is exactly why it’s worth slowing down to see.

For anyone who builds in public, this is not abstract. The moment you ship a thing, make a thing, post a thing, you get reviled — by critics, by competitors, by strangers, by the algorithm’s cruelest 1%. If your stability depends on none of that landing, you’ll be jerked around forever, and worse, you’ll start pre-censoring your work to avoid the sting, which is how builders slowly get boring. The criticism becomes a leash. You stop making the things that might get attacked, which are usually the only things worth making.

Epictetus offers a genuine escape, and it has two moves. First: most criticism is information about the critic, not about you — his fears, his envy, the bad day he’s having, the thing he can’t build himself. Read it that way and it stops being a verdict and becomes a data point about someone else. Second, and harder: where the criticism is true, you don’t need to be wounded — you need to be grateful, because someone just handed you a free defect report. The honest move is to thank the accurate critic and ignore the rest, and the wound disappears in both directions. True or false, there’s nothing left to bleed about.

I want to be precise, because this gets misread as developing a thick skin or not caring. It’s the opposite of not caring — Epictetus cared enormously about doing the work well. The move is to stop outsourcing your sense of the work’s quality to the loudest available stranger. You keep your own honest assessment, you let accurate feedback in through the front door, and you simply decline to let the rest set up residence in your head rent-free. The gate is yours. They can knock; you decide who enters.

So when something stings now, I try to catch the fast invisible step — the ratification — and look at it. Is this true? If yes, fix the thing and thank the source. If no, it’s weather, and weather isn’t an insult, it’s just a fact about the sky. Either way there’s no wound, because I never signed off on one.

They can say it. Only you can make it land.
