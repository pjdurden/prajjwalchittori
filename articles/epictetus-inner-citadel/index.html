The Inner Citadel

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Epictetus had an image the later Stoics loved: the inner citadel. There’s a part of you that no external event can reach unless you open the gate yourself. Take away your money, your reputation, your job, your freedom — Epictetus had lived through losing most of those — and there’s still a room at the center that’s only yours. The whole discipline is learning to live mostly from that room, and to stop building your identity out in the open field where anyone can burn it down.

I think most ambitious people, myself included, build their sense of self in exactly the wrong place. We build it out of externals — the title, the company logo, the follower count, the round we raised, the praise. Then we’re shocked when those things move and take our stability with them. Of course they do. You built the house on land you don’t own. The market gives, the market takes, the algorithm shifts, the funding dries — and if your self was made of those things, your self goes with them.

The citadel is the alternative location. You build your sense of who you are out of what’s actually yours: your judgments, your effort, your character, the quality of your decisions. Those, Epictetus insists, no one can touch without your consent. The launch can fail; whether you built it with integrity is still yours. The deal can collapse; whether you negotiated honestly is still yours. They can take the outcome. They cannot take the workmanship, and the workmanship is where a stable self can actually live.

For a builder this has a sharp practical edge, because building is relentless exposure to outcomes you don’t control. Markets, timing, luck, the whims of users and investors — if your equanimity depends on those going your way, you’ll be jerked around like a flag in wind for your entire career. The engineer who lives in the citadel can take a brutal quarter without coming apart, because the quarter was never where his stability was stored. He keeps it somewhere they can’t reach.

I want to be careful not to make this sound like detachment or not-caring. Epictetus wasn’t passive. He worked, taught, engaged hard with the world. The citadel isn’t a place you hide from life; it’s the foundation you act from — the thing that lets you take big swings precisely because a miss can’t reach the core. You can risk more out in the open field when you know the field isn’t where you live. The citadel is what makes boldness sustainable instead of suicidal.

So the discipline I’m after is a kind of address change. Move your self in off the externals. Keep building, shipping, wanting, swinging — but store the thing that makes you you somewhere the market can’t repossess it. Let the outcomes be outcomes. Let the praise and the criticism both stay outside the gate, where they belong, neither of them allowed all the way in.

Build your empire in the open. Build your self in the keep.
