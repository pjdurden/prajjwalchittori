The Founders Were Deists

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Most of the men who built America didn’t believe in a god who answered prayers. Jefferson took a razor to his Bible and cut out every miracle, keeping only the ethics. Franklin called himself a deist and meant it. Paine wrote The Age of Reason essentially to argue that the universe itself was the only scripture worth reading. They believed in a God who designed a machine and then stepped back to let it run.

I find this the most underrated fact about the founding. These were not mystics. They were engineers of a sort — men who looked at the world as a mechanism with discoverable rules, and who thought the highest form of reverence was to understand the rules rather than beg the operator to break them.

What does a deist actually believe? That the system was set in motion with good design, and that design — not intervention — is where the divinity lives. You don’t pray for the bridge to hold. You build it to hold. The respect you pay the universe is competence.

This is a builder’s theology, and it transfers directly. When you design a protocol, or a company, or a constitution, the deist ambition is the right one: make a thing that runs without you. The amateur builds a system that needs his constant intervention and calls that importance. The deist builds a system so well-designed that his absence is invisible. Jefferson and company were trying to write a machine that would still be running two centuries after every author was dead. They mostly succeeded, which is more than most of us can say about our code.

There’s a humility in it that people miss because they hear “deism” and think “arrogant Enlightenment men.” But the arrogance is the other way around — it’s the interventionist who thinks the system can’t run without him constantly reaching in. The deist sets the rules carefully because he intends to leave. He’s planning his own irrelevance from the first line.

I think about this whenever I’m tempted to make myself necessary. The instinct to be indispensable is the instinct of a small builder. The founders wanted to be dispensable — to design something that didn’t depend on their continued presence, their mood, their being alive. That’s the real ambition. Not to be the hand that keeps reaching in, but to be the mind that made reaching in unnecessary.

Build the clock. Then have the nerve to walk away from it.
