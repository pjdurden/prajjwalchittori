Rehearse The Crash Before You Launch

Prajjwal Chittori · September 2025

The Stoics had a practice that sounds, on first contact, like a recipe for anxiety: premeditatio malorum, the premeditation of evils. You deliberately imagine, in detail, the things going wrong. The deal falling through. The launch flopping. The loss, the failure, the worst case. Epictetus drills it constantly — when you kiss your child, he says, remind yourself she is mortal. Picture the loss while you still have the thing. It sounds morbid. It’s actually the opposite. It’s how you stop being terrified.

Here’s the mechanism, and it’s pure engineering. Fear feeds on the unexamined worst case — the vague, looming, formless catastrophe your mind keeps gesturing at without ever looking directly. The moment you turn and stare at it, draw its actual outline, the fear shrinks, because most worst cases are survivable once specified. The dread was never about the thing. It was about the haze around the thing. Premeditatio malorum dissolves the haze by forcing you to render the disaster in full resolution, and a rendered disaster is almost always smaller than the imagined one.

We already do this professionally, we just don’t call it Stoicism. We call it chaos engineering, failure-mode analysis, the pre-mortem. You sit down before the launch and ask: what are all the ways this dies? You kill the server on purpose to see what breaks. You assume the dependency fails and design for it. No competent engineer ships a critical system without having vividly imagined its failure, because imagining the failure in advance is precisely what lets you build the thing that survives it. We accept this as obvious in code and then refuse to apply it to our own careers and nerves.

The Stoic addition is to run the pre-mortem on your emotional exposure, not just your system’s. Before the launch, I try to actually sit with: what if this fails completely? Play it forward. Where would I be, what would I do, who would I still be. And almost every time, the honest answer is — I’d be fine. Bruised, poorer in one currency, but standing and able to build the next thing. Having genuinely visited that outcome in advance, I find I can walk into the launch without the desperate grip that makes people choke. You can’t be threatened with a place you’ve already been.

There’s a subtler benefit too. Premeditation of evils inoculates you against entitlement. If you’ve pictured losing the thing, you hold it more lightly and appreciate it more while it’s here — both at once. The unexamined good outcome makes you fragile and ungrateful; you assume it’ll keep going, so its arrival means nothing and its loss means everything. The rehearsed loss flips both: the having becomes a gift, and the losing becomes survivable.

So I’ve made it a pre-launch ritual, the way I’d run a load test. Before the thing goes live, I render the crash in full detail and sit in it until it stops being scary. Then I ship from a calm I couldn’t fake otherwise — not because I expect it to fail, but because I’ve already met the version of me that survives if it does.

Look directly at the worst case. It’s almost always smaller up close.
