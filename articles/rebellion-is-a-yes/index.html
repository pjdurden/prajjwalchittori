Rebellion Is a Yes

Prajjwal Chittori · December 2019

We use the word rebel lazily. We picture someone tearing things down, saying no to everything, the angry contrarian who’s just against. Camus spent a whole book correcting this, and the correction is the most useful thing I’ve read about why anyone builds anything.

His claim: every real rebellion starts with a no but runs on a hidden yes. The slave who finally refuses an order is, in the same breath, asserting there’s something in him worth defending. A line, a value, a this far and no further. The no points at the master. The yes points at the thing inside that the no is protecting. You can’t reject something without, underneath, affirming something else. Pure negation is impossible. There’s always a yes funding the no.

Sounds like philosophy until you point it at building, then it’s a diagnostic you can run on yourself.

Plenty of people start companies out of a no. This product is broken. This industry is rotten. These incumbents are lazy. The no is real and usually correct. But a no by itself doesn’t sustain a build, because the second you’ve registered the complaint, the energy’s gone. I’ve watched it happen, including to me. You launch in a fury of this shouldn’t exist and four weeks later the fury’s burned off and there’s nothing underneath, because you never located the yes. The no was a spark with no fuel behind it.

Camus’s test is to find the affirmation under the rejection. Why does the broken thing offend you? What does it violate, what value are you actually defending? Things should work. People deserve a tool that respects them. This is beneath what’s possible. That’s the yes, and the yes is what you build toward once the no burns out. The no gets you off the couch. Only the yes gets you to a second year.

And here it ties back to the absurd, because Camus is consistent. He already told us the universe supplies no meaning. We showed up wanting it and the silence answered. The honest response to that silence isn’t despair and isn’t pretending. It’s rebellion. To affirm a value anyway, in a universe that won’t underwrite it, is the most human act there is. The builder is a rebel in exactly this sense. Someone who insists on a yes, this should exist, this is worth making, in a world that was getting along fine without it and offers zero guarantee it’ll care.

That’s why building is closer to revolt than to optimisation. Optimisation improves what’s there. Revolt asserts what should be there against a reality that didn’t ask. Every venture is a small yes shouted into indifference, and the shout is the point even when the venture dies.

So when you’re deciding what to make, don’t stop at what you’re against. Anyone can be against. Find the thing you’re for that the no was hiding. The value you’d defend with no audience, no payoff, no guarantee. That’s the only fuel that outlasts the spark.

Rebellion isn’t a no. It’s a yes that finally got angry enough to build.
