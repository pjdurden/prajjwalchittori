Rebellion Is a Yes

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

We use the word rebel lazily. We picture someone tearing things down, saying no to everything, the angry contrarian who’s against. Camus spent a whole book correcting this, and his correction is the most useful thing I’ve ever read about why anyone builds anything.

His claim: every real rebellion begins with a no but is powered by a hidden yes. The slave who finally refuses an order is, in the same breath, asserting that there is something in him worth defending — a line, a value, a this far and no further. The no points at the master. The yes points at a thing inside that the no is protecting. You cannot reject something without, underneath, affirming something else. Pure negation is impossible. There’s always a yes funding the no.

This sounds like philosophy until you apply it to building, and then it becomes a diagnostic you can run on yourself.

A lot of people start companies out of a no. This product is broken. This industry is rotten. These incumbents are lazy. The no is real and often correct. But a no by itself doesn’t sustain a build, because the moment you’ve registered your complaint, the energy’s gone. I’ve watched it — including in myself. You launch in a fury of this shouldn’t exist and four weeks later the fury has burned off and there’s nothing underneath, because you never located the yes. The no was a spark with no fuel behind it.

Camus’s test is to find the affirmation under the rejection. Why does the broken thing offend you? What does its existence violate — what’s the value you’re actually defending? Things should work. People deserve a tool that respects them. This is beneath what’s possible. That’s the yes, and the yes is what you build toward after the no runs out. The no gets you off the couch. Only the yes gets you to a second year.

And here’s where it ties back to the absurd, because Camus is consistent. He’s already told us the universe doesn’t supply meaning; we showed up wanting it and the silence answered. The honest response to that silence is not despair and not pretending — it’s rebellion. To affirm a value anyway, in a universe that doesn’t underwrite it, is the most human act there is. The builder is a rebel in exactly this sense: someone who insists on a yes — this should exist, this is worth making — in a world that was getting along fine without it and offers no guarantee it’ll care.

That’s why building is closer to revolt than to optimization. Optimization improves what’s there. Revolt asserts what should be there against a reality that didn’t ask. Every venture is a small yes shouted into indifference, and the shout is the point even when the venture doesn’t survive.

So when you’re deciding what to make, don’t stop at what you’re against. Anyone can be against. Find the thing you’re for that the no was hiding — the value you’d defend even with no audience, no payoff, no guarantee. That’s the only fuel that lasts past the spark.

Rebellion isn’t a no. It’s a yes that finally got angry enough to build.
