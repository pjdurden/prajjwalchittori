Busy Is The Cheapest Way To Feel Productive

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Seneca had a particular contempt for a type of person he called the occupati — the preoccupied, the perpetually busy. Not the lazy; he had less to say about them. His real target was the man in constant motion, slammed every day, running from task to task, who would die having never actually lived because he never stopped long enough to. Seneca’s insight, two thousand years early, is one the modern world still hasn’t absorbed: busyness is not the same as living, and being slammed is not the same as building.

I think busy is the cheapest possible way to feel productive, and that’s exactly why it’s so dangerous. It gives you the sensation of progress — the full calendar, the cleared inbox, the meetings attended, the constant motion — without requiring any of the hard, uncomfortable, slow work that actually moves a thing forward. You can be exhausted every single day and, viewed across a year, have built nothing. The exhaustion fools you. It feels like the receipt of effort, so you assume it must be the receipt of progress. It isn’t.

The real work, in my experience, is almost always the thing that doesn’t generate the busy feeling. It’s sitting with one hard problem until it cracks. It’s the deep, undistracted block where you build the actual thing. It’s the uncomfortable strategic question you keep avoiding by staying productively busy with everything around it. Busy is, among other things, a sophisticated form of procrastination — the most respectable one, because it looks exactly like work and even other people congratulate you for it.

Seneca’s antidote was to be greedy with time and generous with almost nothing else. He’d ask whether a given activity was a genuine claim on his hours or just noise that had successfully dressed itself up as obligation. Most of what fills a busy day, he found, was the latter — things that felt urgent and weren’t important, demands from people who hadn’t earned the time, motion mistaken for meaning. He guarded his hours like a miser guards coins, precisely because everyone around him was so casually spending theirs.

For a builder this maps almost perfectly onto leverage. The busy person does many low-leverage things and feels great about the volume. The effective person does a small number of high-leverage things and often feels lazy, because the high-leverage move is frequently quiet, slow, and unglamorous — a single good decision, one well-designed system, one hard problem genuinely solved. The output is enormous; the visible busyness is low. Our instincts reward the opposite, which is why so many capable people stay stuck in profitable-feeling motion that goes nowhere.

So I’ve learned to be suspicious of my own busyness, especially when it feels good. When I notice I’m slammed, I ask Seneca’s question: am I building, or am I just spending my hours in a way that flatters me? Is this motion, or is this leverage? The busy feeling is not evidence. Sometimes the most productive thing I can do is clear the calendar, disappoint some people, and go be quietly, unimpressively useful on the one thing that actually compounds.

Don’t confuse the receipt of effort with the receipt of progress.
