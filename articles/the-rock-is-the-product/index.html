The Rock Is the Product

Prajjwal Chittori · September 2022

Camus ends his essay on Sisyphus with a line that sounds like a paradox and isn’t: one must imagine Sisyphus happy. A man condemned to roll a boulder uphill forever, watch it roll back down forever, and walk down forever to start again — and we’re meant to imagine him content. People read this as cope. It is the opposite of cope. It’s the only un-deluded position available to a builder.

Here’s the part everyone skips. Camus is not interested in the uphill push. He’s interested in the walk back down. That hour of consciousness, he calls it. The descent, when the rock is gone and Sisyphus is alone with the fact of his situation, fully aware, not lying to himself — that’s where the freedom lives.

Translate it. The uphill push is shipping the thing. The rock at the top, then gone — that’s launch, the dopamine, the screenshot. And the walk back down is the part nobody talks about: the morning after the launch, when the high is gone and the thing just sits there needing maintenance, and you have to decide whether to start again knowing exactly how this ends.

I’ve built five things that didn’t last. For a long time I read that as five failures, five rocks that rolled back down. Then I noticed the rocks were never the point. I don’t actually want a finished, frozen, perfect company sitting motionless at the summit. I’d be bored inside a week. What I want is the pushing. The problem at 2am that’s a punch to the face. The thing that doesn’t exist yet starting to exist under my hands.

Sisyphus’s gods made one error. They assumed the punishment was the labor. But the labor was the only thing that was ever his. They could take the rock away from the top a thousand times; they could never take the pushing. The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart. He owns the verb. They only own the noun.

This is not an argument for futility. It’s an argument against a specific lie — the lie that there’s a summit where the pushing stops and you finally get to rest in the having. There isn’t. There’s the next problem, always, and the only real question is whether you’ve made peace with that or are still waiting to be released from it. The man waiting to be released is miserable at the top and the bottom both. The man who has accepted the structure is, strangely, free — because nothing that happens to the rock can touch him.

When AI arrived, it offered convenience and we offered our lives. Part of what it offered was a fantasy: that the pushing could be automated away, that you could have the summit without the climb. But if the climb was the point, the machine that removes it isn’t doing you a favor. It’s taking your rock.

Keep your rock. Push it on purpose, eyes open, no gods watching. The product was never at the top. The product is your two hands on the stone.
