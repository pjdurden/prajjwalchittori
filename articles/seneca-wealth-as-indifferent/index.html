Hold Money Loosely Or It Holds You

Prajjwal Chittori · September 2025

Seneca is the most useful Stoic on money for one annoying reason: he was rich. Properly, scandalously rich — one of the wealthiest men in Rome — which means when he writes about wealth he isn’t a monk romanticizing a poverty he’s never tasted. He kept the money. He also refused to be owned by it. That combination is the whole lesson, and almost nobody pulls it off.

The Stoics had a word for money: an indifferent. Not bad, not good — neutral. A tool. You’d prefer to have it, the way you’d prefer health over sickness, but your inner state shouldn’t depend on it, because the moment it does, you’ve handed the keys to something outside you. Seneca’s position was: pursue wealth, use it well, enjoy it even — but be ready to lose all of it tomorrow without losing yourself. “I never trusted Fortune,” he wrote, even while she was being generous, “even when she seemed to be at peace.”

I find this the only sane way to want money hard while staying free. Because there are two ways to be owned by money, and most ambitious people only notice one. The obvious way is not having it — scarcity forcing every move. The subtle way is having it and being terrified of losing it. That second man looks free and isn’t. He can’t take the risk, can’t walk from the bad deal, can’t make the bold call, because his identity is now load-bearing on a number. He acquired the money and the money acquired him.

Seneca’s test for whether you actually own your wealth was simple and a little frightening. Periodically, live as if you’d lost it. Eat the plain food, wear the rough clothes, and ask yourself: is this the condition I was so afraid of? He did this on purpose, voluntarily, while rich — not as performance, but as a vaccine. He wanted to face the worst-case version of poverty in a controlled dose, so that the fear of it could never again dictate his decisions. A man who has rehearsed the bottom can’t be threatened with it.

For a builder this is everything, because the whole job is making bets under uncertainty. The engineer who can’t stomach the loss of his comfort makes small, defensive, dying bets. The one who’s genuinely made peace with the downside can size correctly — can hold a position, walk from a deal, build the slow right thing — because he’s not negotiating with terror the whole time. The freedom to play well comes precisely from not needing the chips too badly.

So I want to build wealth, and I’m not embarrassed about it. But I’m trying to hold it the way Seneca held it — tightly enough to use, loosely enough to lose. The aim isn’t to need less money. It’s to need the outcome less, so the decisions stay clean.

You can want the money. Just don’t let it find out how much.
