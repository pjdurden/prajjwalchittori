Franklin Ran A/B Tests on His Own Soul

Prajjwal Chittori · May 2023

Benjamin Franklin wanted to become a better person, so he did the most unromantic thing imaginable. He made a list. Thirteen virtues — temperance, silence, order, resolution, and so on — and a little grid, a week per virtue, a black mark in the box every time he failed. He was, in effect, running instrumentation on himself. Logging events. Watching the dashboard for the metric to go up.

Most people find this faintly ridiculous. Self-improvement as spreadsheet. But I think Franklin understood something that the people who roll their eyes at him do not, which is that character is not a mood. It’s a system, and systems are improved by measurement, not by sincerity.

The deists believed in a clockwork universe — a world of discoverable, regular laws. What people forget is that Franklin turned that lens inward. If the cosmos is a mechanism with rules, so are you. You are not a soul that occasionally misbehaves; you are a process with failure modes, and the failure modes are observable if you bother to observe them. He didn’t pray to be better. He tracked himself to be better. The distance between those two verbs is the whole Enlightenment.

I do a cheap version of this and I’m not embarrassed about it. I count problems solved. I count days I shipped. When I tried to fix my own focus, I didn’t journal about my feelings — I logged when I drifted and looked for the pattern. It turned out my drift had a schedule, the way a flaky test has a schedule. You can’t fix what you won’t measure, and you can’t measure what you’re too proud to write in a box.

Here’s the part people miss. Franklin admitted he never achieved the virtues. Order defeated him for life; he was a brilliant, cluttered man to the end. But he said the attempt made him better than he’d otherwise have been — “a better and happier man than I otherwise should have been if I had not attempted it.” That’s the engineer’s relationship to a metric exactly. You will never hit 100% uptime. The number is there to pull you, not to be reached. A target you achieve was set too low.

The trap is thinking measurement is cold. People imagine the instrumented life is a joyless life, all grids and no grace. But it’s the opposite. The unmeasured life is the one ruled by mood — you feel disciplined on Tuesday and feral on Thursday and have no idea why, because you logged nothing. Franklin’s grid wasn’t a cage. It was a flashlight. It showed him where he actually was instead of where he vaguely felt he was.

So when I want to change something real about how I work or who I am, I don’t start with resolve. Resolve is a feeling, and feelings don’t survive contact with a hard week. I start with a counter. Make the thing visible, give it a number, and let the number do the moralizing you were going to do badly anyway.

Franklin treated himself like a thing he was building. He was right to. You are the longest project you will ever ship, and you don’t even have a log file open.
