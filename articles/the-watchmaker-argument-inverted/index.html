The Watchmaker Argument, Inverted

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

The deists loved the watchmaker argument. Find a watch on a heath, said the analogy, and you’d never believe it assembled itself — the intricacy implies a maker. The universe is more intricate than a watch, therefore: a maker. It’s a famous argument for God’s existence, and modern people mostly file it under “quaint.”

I want to run it backwards, because the inverted version is the most useful thing the deists ever handed a builder.

The original asks: given this intricate machine, what kind of maker made it? Invert it: given that you are a maker, what kind of machine should you make? The watchmaker analogy only flatters the divine because a watch is the kind of thing that, once wound, runs on its own. Nobody imagines the watchmaker standing inside the watch all day pushing the gears. The whole force of the analogy is that good design produces autonomy. A watch you have to hold and turn by hand isn’t a watch. It’s a complicated way to waste a watchmaker.

So here’s the inverted argument, the one I actually live by: the quality of a maker is measured by how little the made thing needs the maker afterward. The deist God is impressive precisely because He stepped back. The system runs without Him. He didn’t make a thing that required His constant attention; that would be a worse God, a needier God, a God who couldn’t design His way to a day off. Omnipotence, in the deist picture, looks like absence. The better the design, the more invisible the designer.

Now look at how most of us build, and feel the indictment. We build watches we have to hold. The service that only the original author can deploy. The process that breaks the moment one person goes on leave. The “system” that is really just a person heroically intervening, over and over, dressed up as architecture. We do this and then we feel important, because the watch needs us. The deists would see that need for what it is: evidence of a bad watchmaker. If the watch needs you to keep pushing the gears, you didn’t finish making the watch.

The inverted watchmaker argument is brutal on ego, which is why I like it. It says your importance to a system is inversely proportional to your skill at building it. The indispensable person is, almost by definition, the one who designed indispensability into the system instead of designing it out. The truly skilled builder is the one nobody has to call. They wound the watch correctly and walked off the heath, and the watch is still keeping time, and they get no daily reminder of their own necessity — because there isn’t any. That absence is the achievement.

I try to measure my own work by this now. Not “how much does this depend on me” — that’s the amateur’s pride — but “how cleanly could I disappear from this.” A thing I built that still runs a year after I stopped touching it is worth more than a thing that needs me every week, even if the second one feels better to my ego. The deist God designed for His own irrelevance and called it perfection.

Make watches, not things you have to hold. Then have the confidence of a good watchmaker, and leave the heath.
