Money Is Stored Freedom

Prajjwal Chittori · April 2022

People who say money doesn’t matter have usually never been without it. People who say money is everything have usually never had enough to find out it isn’t. The truth is in a less quotable place: money is stored freedom. It’s optionality you saved up earlier and can spend later — the ability to say no, to leave, to wait, to choose the slow correct thing over the fast desperate one.

That reframe changes what you’re actually accumulating. If money were status, you’d buy the watch. If money is freedom, you buy the runway — the eighteen months where you don’t have to take the bad deal. The richest thing money buys isn’t a house. It’s the sentence “I don’t need this,” delivered to someone who assumed you did.

I grew up close enough to scarcity to understand it as a structural fact, not a feeling. Scarcity isn’t just having less; it’s having fewer moves. When you’re broke, every choice is forced — you take the job, the rent, the terms, because the alternative is a cliff. Money widens the board. It doesn’t make you happy; happiness is a different department entirely. It makes you free, which is the precondition for going and finding out what would make you happy.

This is also why I think chasing money for status is a category error that quietly ruins people. Status spends the freedom the moment you acquire it — the watch, the car, the lifestyle that now requires the income that bought it. You converted stored freedom back into obligation. You were rich for an afternoon. The discipline is to hold the freedom unspent, to sit on optionality like a deist sits on a well-designed system: not reaching in, just letting the fact of it quietly expand what you’re able to do.

There’s a deism here too, actually. The founders wanted a republic that could run without a king — a system where no one had to ask permission. Personal money is the same machine at human scale. It’s the engine that lets you operate without asking the boss, the landlord, the market for permission to exist next month. You build it precisely so you can stop needing anyone’s hand reaching in.

So I’m unsentimental about it. I want to maximize the stored freedom, and I’m not embarrassed by that, because I know what it’s for. Not the watch. The eighteen months. The ability to walk. The capacity to choose the slow correct thing.

Money is freedom you saved. Spend it on more freedom, not on the appearance of having it.
