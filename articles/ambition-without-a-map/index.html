Ambition Without a Map

Prajjwal Chittori · February 2020

The people who gave me career advice all assumed a map existed. Pick the destination, find the established route, walk it. Become a senior engineer, then a staff engineer, then a director. The ladder is right there; just climb. It’s good advice for the world it was written in. That world is closing.

The map worked because the terrain was stable. The jobs of 2035 were knowable from 2015 — same roles, more zeros, predictable rungs. You could optimize a path because the path would still be there when you arrived. But when the terrain itself is being rewritten every eighteen months — when the agents are eating whole categories of work and creating others nobody has named yet — the map is a liability. You can walk a perfect route to a city that no longer exists.

So how do you have ambition without a map? You stop optimizing for positions and start optimizing for capabilities. A position is a coordinate on a map — “senior engineer at a known company.” When the map redraws, your coordinate can vanish underneath you while you’re standing on it. A capability is something you carry: the ability to take a thing from zero to one, to build distribution, to judge which problem matters, to learn a new domain faster than the people already in it. Capabilities don’t get deprecated when the terrain shifts. They’re how you survive the shift. They travel.

Here’s the reframe that changed how I move. Without a map, you can’t navigate, but you can still take bearings. A bearing isn’t “get to this city.” It’s a direction — “toward more leverage,” “toward owning more of the value I create,” “toward problems the agents can’t yet touch.” You can’t plan the whole route, because the route doesn’t exist yet, but you can make sure every individual step points roughly the right way. You navigate by the next step’s direction, not by a destination you’ve memorized. It’s less comforting than a map. It’s also the only thing that works when the map is a lie.

This is harder than it sounds because ambition wants a destination. It wants a finish line to sprint at. Bearing-based ambition feels worse, because you never get to point at a spot and say there, that’s where I’m going, that’s when I’ll have made it. You’re permanently in motion with no announced arrival. But that ache for a fixed destination is exactly the thing the old map exploited, and it’s why people cling to dead ladders long after the rungs rotted out.

The founders I keep coming back to didn’t have a map either. There was no precedent for the thing they were building — no prior republic of that shape to copy. They had bearings. Liberty, reason, self-governance — directions, not destinations, and they navigated toward them one impossible decision at a time, inventing the route as the ground moved under them.

Stop asking where you’ll be in ten years. Nobody knows what will exist in ten years. Ask instead: does my next step point toward more capability and more leverage? If yes, take it, and take the next bearing from wherever you land.
