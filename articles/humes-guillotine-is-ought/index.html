Hume's Guillotine and the Metric That Lies

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2017

David Hume noticed something so simple that for centuries nobody had said it out loud. He observed that writers would reason along comfortably about what is — how the world works, what happens, what causes what — and then, without warning, slip into talking about what ought to be. One sentence about facts, the next about duty, and no bridge between them. He pointed out that you cannot derive an ought from an is. No pile of facts, however tall, tells you on its own what you should do. This is Hume’s guillotine, and it has separated more heads from more bad arguments than any blade in history.

I think about it most when I’m staring at a dashboard.

A metric is an is. It says: engagement is up, latency is down, this number moved. That’s a fact about the world, and facts are good. The error — the error everyone makes, the error whole companies are built on — is sliding silently from the is of the metric to the ought of the decision. “Engagement is up” becomes “therefore we should do more of this,” and nobody notices that a value judgment got smuggled in between two sentences. The metric measured what is. It said nothing about what ought. The “ought” was your goal, your value, your bet on what matters — and the metric quietly took credit for a decision it never made.

This is how you get optimization disasters. You pick a proxy metric because it’s measurable, the metric goes up, and you treat the rising number as a moral verdict. But the number is just an is. The ad revenue is up and the product is getting worse, and these are both facts, and the dashboard cannot adjudicate between them because adjudicating requires an ought that lives outside the data. Hume’s guillotine is the discipline of remembering that your metrics describe the world and your values decide what to do about it, and that the second can never be outsourced to the first.

The deists and the skeptics around them were obsessed with this boundary because they were trying to build ethics on reason rather than revelation. And Hume, the most ruthless of them, basically said: careful — reason can tell you what is, beautifully, but the ought has to come from somewhere reason alone doesn’t reach. He wasn’t being nihilistic. He was being precise about jurisdiction. Facts are one court. Values are another. Stop letting the first pretend to rule on the second.

For a builder this is enormously practical. It means: choose your ought deliberately, before you choose your metric, because the metric will never choose it for you and will happily pretend it did. What are we actually trying to be true about the world? That’s a values question, an ought, and it has to be answered in words by humans who are willing to be wrong out loud. Only then do you go find the is — the metric that tells you whether you’re getting there. Pick the metric first and you’ll spend years optimizing a number that was never connected to anything you cared about, wondering why winning feels like losing.

The dashboard is full of is. Your job is the ought. Hume drew the line. Stop letting your metrics step over it.
