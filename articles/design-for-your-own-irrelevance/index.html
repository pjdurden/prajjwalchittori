Design for Your Own Irrelevance

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

The deist God’s most impressive act was leaving. He built the machine, set the laws, and then — this is the part that scandalized everyone — He stepped back and didn’t intervene again. No miracles, no reaching in, no daily adjustments. The design was so complete that intervention became unnecessary. The absence was the achievement.

I want to argue that this is the correct goal for every system you build, every team you lead, and eventually your own role — and that almost everyone’s instinct runs exactly opposite.

The natural human instinct is to make yourself necessary. We feel safest when we’re indispensable: when the deploy needs us, when the answer lives only in our head, when the team falls apart without us in the room. We call this importance, and we protect it, sometimes without admitting we’re protecting it. The hoarded knowledge, the undocumented process, the “just ask me.” It feels like security. It’s actually the opposite — it’s a cage you built and decorated to look like a throne.

Because a person who is indispensable cannot move. That’s the trap nobody tells you about. If the system can’t run without you, then you can’t leave it — can’t take the better role, can’t start the new thing, can’t even take a real vacation, because the moment you step away the thing you made your identity starts to fail. You optimized for being needed, and you succeeded, and now your success is your chain. The indispensable person isn’t powerful. They’re trapped by a system that was designed — by them — to require them.

The deist move is the reverse, and it takes more confidence, not less. You design the system so well that it doesn’t need you. You write the thing down. You build the runbook. You make your own knowledge redundant on purpose. You engineer your way out of the critical path. And yes, this feels like dismantling your own importance — that’s exactly what it is, and that’s exactly why it’s the harder, higher-status thing to do. The amateur makes himself necessary. The master makes himself optional, and then walks to the next thing, because he can.

I’ve come to treat “could I disappear from this cleanly?” as the real measure of whether I built something well. Not “do they need me” — the amateur’s metric, the one that feels good — but “could I vanish and would it keep running.” Every time the answer is no, I haven’t finished building; I’ve just built a system that takes me hostage. The work isn’t done when it runs. The work is done when it runs without me.

There’s a career-shaped version of this too, and it’s the whole engine of moving up. You get to take on bigger things only in proportion to how cleanly you can let go of the current thing. The engineer who can’t be replaced on their service cannot be promoted off it — they’ve made themselves too valuable to move, which is just a flattering way of saying stuck. The deist designs for his own irrelevance precisely so he’s free to go build the next universe. Indispensability is a local maximum dressed up as a summit.

Build the machine. Set the laws. Then have the nerve the deist God had — step back, leave it running, and walk toward the next thing you haven’t built yet. Your irrelevance to the old work is the proof you’re ready for the new.
