The Engineer-Philosopher

Prajjwal Chittori · March 2026

People think it’s strange that the same person solves eight thousand competitive programming problems and reads Kafka and the Stoics for fun. They treat engineering and philosophy as two unrelated hobbies that happen to share a skull. I think they’re the same activity pointed at two different scales.

Engineering is the discipline of being exactly right about small things. A distributed system doesn’t care about your intentions or your vibe. It has invariants, and either you hold them or money disappears and a pager goes off at 3am. Years of this trains a specific reflex: the refusal to let a comfortable belief survive contact with what actually happens. You learn, in your hands, that the world has a truth independent of your opinion of it, and that your job is to find that truth, not to win an argument about it.

Philosophy, done honestly, is the same reflex aimed at the questions too big to put in a test harness. How should I live. What’s worth wanting. What do I actually believe versus what did I inherit and never check. Most people never run these as real queries — they accept the defaults their environment shipped them, the way you’d ship to prod without reading the code. The engineer’s instinct is to crack it open. Why is this here? What happens if I remove it? Is this assertion actually true or is it just load-bearing because nobody dared touch it?

This is exactly what the American founding deists did, and it’s why I keep returning to them. Jefferson took a razor to the Bible and cut out everything he couldn’t reason his way to, keeping only the ethics and discarding the miracles. Paine wrote The Age of Reason applying plain logic to inherited belief and got hated for it. These weren’t men rejecting meaning. They were debugging it — refusing to run code they hadn’t read, even the code everyone around them treated as sacred and untouchable.

The intersection produces something I find nowhere else: the ability to hold a system and its purpose in the same thought. A pure engineer builds the thing right but rarely asks if it’s the right thing — give them a spec and they’ll optimize a machine that shouldn’t exist. A pure philosopher asks what’s worth doing but can’t build it, so the answer stays a vapor. The engineer-philosopher builds the right thing right. They can ask why are we even doing this in the morning and ship the answer by evening. In an age where the building half is being automated away, the why half is exactly the part that’s about to become the whole job.

Reading philosophy makes me a better engineer, and not as a metaphor. It’s the same muscle — examine the assumption, trace it to its root, refuse the inherited default, accept only what survives scrutiny. I just sometimes point it at a database and sometimes at my own life. The tragedy is people who’ll rigorously debug their code and then run their entire life on settings they never once opened. They’ll spend a week chasing a race condition and zero seconds asking whether the thing the code does is worth doing.

Examine the system. All of them. Especially the one running you.
