What Paul Graham Got Right (and the One Place I Disagree)

Prajjwal Chittori · November 2017

If you build things, Paul Graham has already had most of your good ideas a decade before you, written them more clearly than you will, and put them somewhere you can find them for free. The honest thing is to admit the debt up front.

The thing he got most right — the one that took me years to feel in my hands rather than just nod at — is do things that don’t scale. The whole instinct of a technical person is to automate first, to build the machine before there’s anything for it to do. PG’s point is that the unscalable things — recruiting users one by one, doing the work manually, embarrassing yourself in someone’s inbox — are not a phase you tolerate before the real work. They are the real work, early on. They’re how you learn what the machine should even do.

The second thing he got right is the maker’s schedule versus the manager’s. A maker needs a long uninterrupted block or the day is dead. A single meeting at 2pm doesn’t cost you an hour; it costs you the whole afternoon, because it cuts the runway in half from both ends. I’ve reorganized my entire life around protecting those blocks, and everything good I’ve built happened inside one.

Here’s where I disagree, and it’s not small. PG’s worldview assumes the goal is to grow — to take the thing from one person to many, hire, scale, become a company. The entire YC machine is built on that assumption. But there’s a kind of builder he undercounts: the one who wants to build excellent things alone and never wants employees, never wants a board, never wants the thing to become a job for forty people. For that builder, “growth” past a certain point isn’t success — it’s the moment the work stops being the work.

I’ve started several things. The pattern in me isn’t a failure to scale; it’s a preference for the zero-to-one and an allergy to the one-to-a-hundred. PG would read that as a bug. I’ve come to read it as a spec. Not everyone is trying to build a rocket. Some of us are trying to build the best possible thing, ship it, and move to the next idea before the org chart shows up.

So: take the unscalable advice, guard the maker’s schedule, owe him the debt. But don’t let the YC frame convince you that staying small is failing. Sometimes the most ambitious thing is to keep your hands directly on the work and refuse to manage anyone, including the version of yourself that wants to be a CEO.
