Cicero On Why You Do The Work Well When No One Is Watching

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Near the end of his life, with the Roman republic collapsing around him, Cicero wrote a long letter to his son about duty — De Officiis, On Duties. It became one of the most influential books in Western history; the American founders practically memorized it. Its central claim is one that a results-obsessed person like me had to wrestle with, because at first it sounds naive. Cicero argues that the useful and the honorable are never actually in conflict. What seems advantageous but is dishonorable only appears advantageous. The shortcut that requires you to betray the craft was never really a shortcut.

I’ll be honest about why this is hard. In building, the dishonorable shortcut constantly looks like the smart move. Cut the corner nobody will see. Ship the thing you know is fragile. Make the claim that’s technically defensible but actually misleading. The market often seems to reward exactly this, at least in the short window you can see. Cicero’s bet — and it’s a bet — is that over a long enough run, the corner you cut comes back, and the reputation you spent buys less than it cost.

Here’s where I’ve come to think he’s right, and it’s not for soft reasons. It’s because of compounding. A career is a repeated game. The engineer who does the work well when no one is watching builds a thing called trust, and trust is the highest-leverage asset in any long game, because it lets people hand you bigger and bigger surfaces without checking. The one who cuts corners is optimizing a single round of a game that has a thousand rounds. He wins the hand and slowly loses the table.

Cicero ties duty to role — what you owe given who you are and where you stand. The duty of an engineer is to the correctness of the system. The duty of a founder is to the people who trusted the thing. These aren’t sentimental obligations; they’re the load-bearing walls of anything that lasts. You can violate them and get away with it for a while. But the structures built on violated duty have a way of coming down at the worst possible moment, usually when you’ve scaled them large enough that the collapse really hurts.

The part I find most useful is that Cicero locates honor in the craft itself, not in being seen. The work done well in private is the real thing; the applause is downstream and incidental. This flips the usual incentive. Most people do good work to be seen doing it, which means the moment no one’s looking, the quality quietly drops. Cicero’s builder does it well because that’s what the role demands, audience or none — and that’s exactly the person you can hand the keys to.

So I’ve stopped treating integrity as the soft, optional layer on top of the hard work of winning. Cicero convinced me it is the hard work of winning, just measured on a clock most people are too impatient to read. The honorable and the advantageous converge. You just have to play long enough to see them meet.

Do it right when no one’s watching. That’s the whole moat.
