Distribution Is the Only Moat You Have Alone

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Engineers love to believe the product is the moat. Build something good enough and the world beats a path to your door. It’s a comforting lie, and I believed it through several dead projects before it stopped being free to believe.

Here’s the thing nobody tells you when you’re a strong technical person building alone: your product is not defensible. Anything one good engineer can build, another good engineer can clone in a weekend, and the agents will soon clone in an afternoon. The code is not the moat. The code is the table stakes. Whatever clever architecture you’re proud of, assume it’s commoditized by Tuesday.

So what’s left? For a solo builder, exactly one thing is durable: the fact that people already listen to you. Distribution. The audience that opens the message, the followers who see the post, the inbox that’s already warm. That’s the one asset that doesn’t get cloned over a weekend, because it took years to build and it’s attached to you, not to your repo.

I learned this from the side of my life that has nothing to do with backend systems. I built an audience of twenty-thousand-plus people on short-form philosophy — one format, repeated, for long enough that it compounded. Nobody can fork that. They can copy my exact script and post it from a zero account and it dies on arrival, because the distribution isn’t in the words. It’s in the years of showing up that taught an algorithm and a few thousand humans to give me a first second of attention. That first second is the moat. Everything downstream is just product.

This inverts how most engineers spend their time, and the inversion is the whole point. We pour ninety percent of our effort into the product and treat distribution as something gross you do at the end, once the thing is “ready.” That’s backwards. The product is the easy half. The hard, slow, compounding, un-cloneable half is getting anyone to care, and you should have been building it the whole time the product didn’t exist yet.

Paul Graham says do things that don’t scale — recruit your first users by hand. He’s right, but I’d push it further. Building distribution is the unscalable thing, and unlike recruiting your first ten users, it doesn’t end. You build distribution every day you publish, for free, before you have anything to sell. By the time you have something to sell, the channel is already warm. People who skip this step spend launch day screaming into a void and call it bad luck.

If you’re a solo builder, here’s the order of operations I wish someone had forced on me earlier: build the audience, then build the product the audience tells you it wants. Not the other way around. The product is a commodity. The attention is the asset.

Code is cheap and getting cheaper. Trust took years and can’t be downloaded. Guard the thing that took years.
