Build For The Spec You Were Given

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2019

Marcus had a habit of accepting his materials. “Accept the things to which fate binds you,” he wrote, “and love the people with whom fate brings you together — but do so with all your heart.” The Stoics later sharpened this into amor fati — not just tolerating what happens, but loving it, working with the hand you were dealt instead of resenting that it wasn’t a better hand.

The reason this is hard for ambitious people is that we’re trained to imagine the ideal conditions and then feel cheated by the real ones. The ideal cofounder, the ideal market timing, the ideal amount of capital, the ideal background. We build a fantasy version of the situation we should have been handed, and then we treat the actual situation as a defect to complain about rather than the thing to build with. Marcus would say: the cards are the cards. The only question worth your energy is how well you play these ones.

I think of it in engineering terms as building for the spec you were actually given, not the spec you wish you’d been given. Every real system comes with constraints you didn’t choose — legacy code, hard latency budgets, a weird requirement from a customer who pays the bills, a platform that does things its own way. The junior instinct is to resent the constraints and design for an imaginary clean-slate world. The senior move is to treat the constraints as the problem, because they are. Anyone can build the thing under ideal conditions. The skill is the actual conditions. The constraints are the job.

Amor fati takes this further than mere acceptance, and that’s the part I find genuinely powerful. It says: don’t just tolerate the constraint, use it. The latency budget that seemed like a curse forces an architecture that turns out to be your edge. The lack of capital forces a discipline that the well-funded competitor never develops and can’t fake. The weird background you were embarrassed by turns out to be the exact angle nobody else can see from. The thing you’d have edited out of your story is frequently the thing that makes the story work.

This is not the same as pretending everything happens for a good reason — I don’t believe that, and Marcus, running an empire through war and plague, certainly didn’t. The universe doesn’t owe you a tidy plot. Amor fati isn’t a claim that the cards are secretly good. It’s a discipline about where you point your effort: at playing this hand superbly, rather than at grieving the hand you didn’t get. The grief is pure waste. The play is everything.

So when I catch myself resenting my conditions — wishing the timing were different, the resources bigger, the background cleaner — I try to run the Marcus move. These are the materials. Not the materials I’d have ordered. The ones on the bench. Love them enough to build something excellent out of them, because they’re the only ones I actually have, and the fantasy version was never going to ship anything.

Stop designing for the spec you wanted. Build the one you got.
