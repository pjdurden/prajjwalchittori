The Clockwork Universe Already Survived This Panic

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

When the deists started describing the universe as a clockwork mechanism — lawful, predictable, running on its own without a hovering God — a lot of people panicked. If the world is just a machine following rules, where’s the meaning? Where’s the human? If it all runs deterministically, what’s left for us? It felt like the mechanism was going to swallow the soul. People genuinely believed that proving the universe was a clock would empty human life of significance.

It didn’t. And I keep noticing that the AI panic is the same panic, beat for beat, and that the deists already lived through the resolution.

Here’s the structure of both fears. A new picture of the world arrives in which a mechanism does things we thought required a special human spark. For the deists it was the cosmos running itself without divine intervention — the spark of God seemingly written out. For us it’s machines doing the cognition we thought was uniquely ours — the spark of human intelligence seemingly written out. Both times the fear is identical: if the machine can do it, what was I? And both times the fear assumes that the value of the thing lived in its being scarce and mysterious, rather than in anything else.

The deists’ resolution was not to deny the clockwork. They didn’t pretend the universe wasn’t mechanical to protect human meaning. They did something braver: they accepted the mechanism and relocated the human role inside it. The dignity wasn’t in the universe being magic. It was in being the kind of mind that could understand the mechanism, work with its laws, and build on top of it. The clock didn’t erase the watchmaker’s significance — it gave the watchmaker something worthy of his skill. You don’t lose meaning when the world becomes legible. You gain a place to apply yourself.

I think that’s the whole answer to the AI question, and I say this as someone who is genuinely AI-anxious, who has felt the pickpocket fear at 2am. The mistake is assuming your value lived in doing the cognition that the machine now does. It didn’t, any more than the deist’s value lived in personally lighting the stars. Your value is in being the mind that understands the new mechanism well enough to direct it, build on it, and aim it at something. When AI arrived it offered convenience, and a lot of people will hand over their whole lives for it the way they’d hand over a limb for a crutch. But the deist response isn’t to refuse the mechanism. It’s to refuse the passivity — to be the one who understands the clock, not the one paralyzed by the discovery that it ticks on its own.

The clockwork universe turned out to be the most generative idea in history, not the death of meaning everyone feared. It launched physics, engineering, the entire modern world — because once people accepted the mechanism, they could finally build with it instead of praying at it. The mechanism wasn’t the end of human significance. It was the beginning of human leverage. Every machine that does something we used to do by hand has, eventually, freed that hand for something larger.

We’re at the same threshold the deists were, feeling the same vertigo, asking the same question about what’s left for us. The answer is what it was last time: understand the machine, work its laws, build on top, and locate your significance in directing the mechanism rather than in being the only thing that could do its work. The clockwork didn’t empty the universe. It handed us the controls. So will this.
