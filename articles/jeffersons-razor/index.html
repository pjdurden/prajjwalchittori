Jefferson Took a Razor to His Bible

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Thomas Jefferson literally cut up the New Testament. He sat with a blade and physically excised every miracle, every resurrection, every supernatural claim, and pasted what remained — the ethics, the sayings, the actual teaching — into a slim book now called the Jefferson Bible. He kept Jesus the moral philosopher and threw away Jesus the magician. He thought the wisdom was real and the special effects were noise burying it.

I don’t bring this up to argue theology. I bring it up because cutting is the rarest and most valuable engineering skill, and almost nobody is brave enough to do it.

Every system you inherit is a holy text. There’s the feature someone fought for in 2019, the abstraction a respected ex-colleague was proud of, the config flag nobody dares delete because what if. Codebases accrete reverence. The longer something exists, the more it gets treated as load-bearing simply because it’s old. And so systems get heavier and heavier, not because anyone added value, but because nobody had the nerve Jefferson had — to look at a revered object and say: most of this is noise, and the signal is small, and I am going to keep only the signal.

The deists were doing subtraction as a method. Paine, Jefferson, Franklin — their whole move was to strip inherited tradition down to what reason could actually defend, and discard the rest no matter how sacred it had become. That’s not vandalism. That’s refactoring. You keep the invariant that survives scrutiny and you delete the cruft that only survives habit.

The reason this is hard isn’t technical. It’s social and psychological. Deleting code feels like destroying value, while adding code feels like creating it — even when the truth is reversed. A pull request that removes 4,000 lines and a feature flag is one of the most valuable things an engineer can produce, and it will get a fraction of the praise of a flashy new service that adds surface area we’ll be maintaining for a decade. We reward accretion and quietly punish subtraction. Jefferson did the unrewarded thing. He made the book smaller and that’s what made it sharper.

I try to keep a Jefferson’s razor on my own work. When I look at something I built a year ago, the question isn’t “what can I add.” It’s “what here is actually carrying weight, and what is just here because I was once proud of it.” The miracle you cut is usually your own old cleverness — the abstraction you were so pleased with that you can no longer see it isn’t earning its keep.

There’s a deeper point under the engineering one. Most people’s lives are like an un-razored Bible: a thick accumulation of commitments, subscriptions, half-projects, opinions, and obligations they kept because removing them felt like loss. Almost none of it carries the actual teaching. The discipline of the blade — keep what reason defends, cut what only nostalgia defends — is as useful on a calendar as on a codebase.

Jefferson believed the truth was small and clean and buried under centuries of decoration. He may have been wrong about the Bible. He was exactly right about almost everything humans build. The signal is small. Have the nerve to cut to it.
