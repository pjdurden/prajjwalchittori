Read Reality As It Is, Not As It Should Be

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2018

The most useful sentence in The Prince is one most people misread. Machiavelli says he writes about how men actually live, not how they ought to live — and that a ruler who insists on the ought will be destroyed by the men who deal in the is. People hear this and think he’s recommending cruelty. He isn’t. He’s recommending sight.

This is a debugging principle before it’s a political one. Every engineer learns it the hard way. You have a mental model of how the system should behave, and you stare at the code, and the code agrees with you, and the bug stays. The bug lives in the gap between your model and the machine. The only way out is to drop the should and instrument the is — log the actual value, watch the actual packet, read the actual heap. Reality doesn’t care about your design doc.

Most career pain is the same gap. You think your manager should reward the hardest problem solved. In fact he rewards the problem he personally felt the pain of. You think the market should pay for the elegant product. In fact it pays for the one that removed a specific person’s specific Tuesday-morning misery. The builder who keeps losing is usually not lazy or unskilled. He’s just optimizing against a fantasy of how the world works and getting surprised every quarter.

Machiavelli’s word for the skill is virtù — not virtue in the moral sense, but a kind of clear-eyed competence, the ability to act effectively on the world as it presents itself. Paired with it is fortuna, luck, the half of outcomes you don’t control. His claim is that virtù is how you build a wall against fortuna. You can’t stop the flood, but you can read the river and build before the rains. The man who refuses to see the river because rivers shouldn’t flood through his town gets washed away with his principles intact and useless.

Now the part where I’m careful, because this cluster gets misused. Seeing reality clearly is not permission to behave badly. It’s the opposite. The person who refuses to see how power actually moves in their org is precisely the person who gets used by someone who does — and then has no integrity to protect because they had no leverage to protect it with. You keep your principles by being literate in the game, not naive about it. Naivety isn’t innocence. Naivety is just unpreparedness with good PR.

So the discipline is this. Twice a day, catch yourself using the word “should” about other people, the market, your company, the world. Each time, force the swap. Not they should value this — what do they actually value, and is there an honest version of my work that gives it to them? Not this shouldn’t be hard — it is hard, what does that tell me about the terrain?

You don’t lower your standards. You aim them at the real target instead of the imaginary one. That’s the whole edge. Most people are firing at a world that doesn’t exist.
