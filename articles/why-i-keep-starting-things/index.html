Why I Keep Starting Things

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

I’ve started a lot of things. A cleaning brand that did two lakh in its first month. A trading simulator that got featured by YC’s Startup School. A couple of products that exist only as graveyards in my old repos. The pattern is obvious to anyone watching, and the obvious read is that I have a problem with finishing. I want to argue the read is wrong, or at least incomplete.

Starting a thing teaches you something that no amount of studying the thing can. The market doesn’t talk to spectators. It only talks to people who have something at stake — who’ve shipped, taken money, taken the public risk of being wrong in front of others. Every time I start something, I’m not just trying to build a business. I’m buying information that’s only for sale to participants. You cannot read your way to it. You have to pay the entry fee, which is the starting.

Here’s the non-obvious part. Most of what I’ve learned about distribution, pricing, what people actually pay for versus what they say they want, how a brand earns its first second of trust — none of it came from the projects that “worked.” It came from the act of starting, repeatedly, and watching reality respond. The starts that didn’t last weren’t failures to finish. They were experiments that returned their data and got shut down, which is exactly what experiments are supposed to do. We just have an ugly word for it when it’s a business and a clean word for it when it’s a lab.

The world has one narrative for the serial starter, and it’s a scold: you lack discipline, you chase novelty, real builders commit. There’s truth in it I won’t dodge — some things only get great if you stay for the boring decade, and the serial starter rules those out. I’ve written about that cost honestly elsewhere and I’m not relitigating it. But the scold misses the other ledger entirely. Each start compounds you. You get better at the zero-to-one every single time — faster at building, sharper at reading a market, calmer about the early chaos, quicker to find the wedge. The projects are disposable. The operator who started them is not.

Think about what a start actually demands, all at once: pick a problem, judge a market, build the thing, find the first users, set a price, take real money from real strangers. That’s the entire surface area of business compressed into a few months, and you run the whole loop yourself. Do that five times and you’ve trained on more complete cycles than someone who spent five years inside one slow company touching one slice of one of those skills.

Earnestness is part of it too — Paul Graham’s word for it, and the right one. I’m not starting things to seem like a founder. I start because the questions I most want answered are the kind only reality will answer, and reality charges admission, and the price of admission is to begin.

So yes, I keep starting things. Each one is tuition. I’ve gotten very good at the thing I keep paying to learn.
