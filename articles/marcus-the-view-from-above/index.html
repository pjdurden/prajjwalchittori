The View From Above

Prajjwal Chittori · August 2016

Marcus Aurelius had a trick he used when things got loud. He’d zoom out. Picture the whole earth from a great height — the cities, the wars, the marriages, the festivals — and watch how small the thing that was eating him became from up there. He called it the view from above. He was running an empire and fighting a plague while he did it, so it wasn’t a yoga pose. It was load-bearing.

I do a version of this during incidents. When a service is down and the dashboards are red and six people are typing in the channel, the natural state is tunnel vision — your whole nervous system collapses to the size of one stack trace. The view from above is the deliberate act of pulling the camera back. How bad is this, really, on the scale of the company? On the scale of the year? Will anyone remember it in a quarter? Usually the honest answer is: it’s a bad hour, not a bad life. And that answer is what lets your hands stop shaking enough to actually fix it.

Here’s the non-obvious part. The view from above isn’t for making things feel unimportant. It’s for making them the right size. Most panic is a scaling error. You’re feeling a planet-sized emotion about a parking-ticket-sized event, and the feeling, not the event, is what wrecks you. Marcus wasn’t pretending the plague didn’t matter. He was refusing to let his sense of proportion get hijacked by whatever was closest to his face.

Builders need this more than emperors did, because we live closer to the dopamine. A bad launch feels like the end. A competitor’s announcement feels like a knife. A rejected PR, a dropped deal, a feature that flopped — at face-size, each one is a catastrophe. From above, it’s one data point in a long series, most of which you’ll forget. The career that survives is the one whose owner kept rescaling, refusing to spend a year’s worth of dread on a week’s worth of problem.

There’s a darker, more useful version of the zoom too. Pull the camera back far enough and you see your own ending in the frame. Marcus did this constantly — you could leave life right now. Not as morbidity. As editing. From that height, the politics you were obsessing over shrink, and the one or two things that actually matter get loud. The view from above is a filter for triage. It tells you what to drop.

So I’ve made it a practice, not a mood. When I notice the tunnel forming — the heart rate, the narrowing — I run the zoom on purpose. Up to the team. Up to the year. Up to the whole short span of the thing. Then I come back down to the one stack trace with my proportion restored, and I fix it like it’s an hour, because it is.

The skill isn’t staying calm. It’s staying the right size.
