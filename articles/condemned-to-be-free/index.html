Condemned to Be Free

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

Sartre has a phrase that sounds like a contradiction: we are condemned to be free. Condemned — like a sentence, a punishment. Why would freedom be a punishment? Because, he says, we didn’t choose to exist, and yet once we’re here, every single thing we do is a choice we’re responsible for, with no excuse available. There’s no human nature handed down to blame, no script, no author above us who decided in advance what we’d be. We’re thrown into existence and then forced to invent ourselves, with the bill for every choice coming straight to us.

People hear radical freedom and think it sounds liberating, even fun. Sartre knew it was mostly terrifying, and that most of us spend our lives fleeing it. We want to be free in the abstract and unfree in the particular — we want options, but we want someone else to be responsible for which one we took.

Watch how this plays out in a career. The most common move I see, in myself and everyone, is to outsource the authorship. The market decided. The roadmap said. My manager set the priorities. This is just what people in my role do. Each of these is true and each is, in Sartre’s sense, a small lie — because you chose to be in the market, on the roadmap, under the manager, in the role. The constraints are real, but you selected the cage and you renew the lease every morning. Pretending the cage chose you is the comfortable fiction that lets you off the hook.

Sartre won’t let you off. His doctrine is almost cruel in its refusal of excuses: you are nothing but the sum of your actions. Not your intentions, not your potential, not the great thing you would have built under better conditions. Just what you actually did. The novel you meant to write is not a novel. The company you would have started in a fairer world is not a company. There is no credit for the unbuilt — existence precedes essence, you are made entirely out of your acts, and the acts you didn’t take are simply not part of you.

This sounds harsh and it’s the most motivating idea I know. Because the flip side of total responsibility is total authorship. If nothing was decided in advance, then nothing is fixed. The role doesn’t own you. The pattern you’ve followed isn’t destiny. The four-month engine, the past abandonments, the credential you do or don’t have — none of it is an essence; it’s all just prior actions, and the next action is unwritten. You’re condemned to keep authoring, which means you’re never finished, which means the disappointing chapter is never the last one unless you stop writing.

When AI arrived, it offered convenience, and the deepest convenience it offers is the same old escape — the model decided, the system optimized, it wasn’t really me. Sartre would call that the purest bad faith yet: handing your authorship to a machine so you never have to own the page. The freedom is still yours. You can always still choose. The machine can generate options; only you can take responsibility for one, and taking responsibility is the whole of what it means to be a person rather than a process.

You didn’t ask to be here. But you’re the author now, with no co-signer and no one to blame.
