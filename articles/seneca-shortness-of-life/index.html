Time Is The Only Thing You Can Go Bankrupt On

Prajjwal Chittori · January 2022

Seneca wrote a short, brutal essay called On the Shortness of Life, and the thesis is not the one the title makes you expect. He doesn’t say life is short. He says we make it short. “It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste much of it.” We’re handed a fortune in time and then we hand it out carelessly to anyone who asks, and at the end we act surprised that we’re broke.

The line that has never left me: we guard our money obsessively, but when it comes to time — the one thing it’s actually respectable to be greedy about — we’re absurdly generous. We’ll fight over a small bill and then give away an entire afternoon to someone who didn’t even ask well. Seneca’s point is that this is backwards. Money you can earn again. Lost time has no refund window. It’s the only account where every withdrawal is permanent.

I take this literally as a builder. Time is the single non-renewable input in everything I make. Capital can be raised, talent can be hired, attention can be bought, code can be rewritten — but the hours I spent are gone whether the thing worked or not. Which means the real cost of a bad project isn’t the money. It’s the months. When I abandon something, the loss I actually mourn is the calendar, not the bank balance, because the calendar is the one I can’t refill.

This reframes “busy” entirely. Seneca was savage about the busy. He thought the preoccupied man — running errands, attending to everyone, perpetually in motion — was the most wasteful of all, because he’d confused activity with living. You can be fully booked and completely bankrupt. I see this in engineering all the time: people who are slammed every single day and, if you zoom out a year, built nothing. They spent the fortune in coins so small they never noticed it leaving.

So I’ve gotten ruthless about a specific question: is this hour buying me something that compounds, or am I just spending it? Meetings rarely compound. Skill compounds. Building something real compounds. Anxiety, scrolling, arguing with strangers, polishing things nobody will see — pure outflow, no return. The discipline isn’t doing more. It’s refusing the withdrawals that don’t buy anything.

There’s a deeper Seneca move here too. He says the person who lives well is the one who lives now — who isn’t perpetually postponing life to some future state where the conditions are finally right. The startup that exits, the number that’s finally enough, the someday. Seneca would say: you’re spending real present time buying an imaginary future, and the exchange rate is terrible. The future you’re saving for is built out of the only currency you have, and you’re handing it over today for a promise.

I don’t think the answer is to stop building. I think the answer is to build the way Seneca wanted us to live — deliberately, greedily protective of the hours, treating each one as a withdrawal from an account that never refills.

Be cheap with your time. It’s the only money that’s really yours.
