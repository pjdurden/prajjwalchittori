The Job-Versus-Potential Trap

Prajjwal Chittori · December 2020

I once wrote a line I keep coming back to because it diagnosed me before I understood the diagnosis: when I have a job, it feels like I’m wasting my potential; when I’m unemployed, it feels like I’m not grinding hard enough. Both states are guilt. There is no third state where I feel fine. That’s the trap, and for years I thought the trap was about jobs. It isn’t.

Here’s the insight that took me too long. The discomfort doesn’t come from the job. It comes from a number you carry around called your potential — an imagined ceiling of everything you could theoretically become — and you measure every present moment against it. The ceiling is always higher than the floor you’re standing on. So you always feel short. A job feels like settling because the ceiling is higher than your title. Unemployment feels like failing because the ceiling is higher than your output. The ceiling wins either way. It’s designed to.

The cruel part is that “potential” feels like ambition’s friend. It feels like the thing driving you to be more. But undefined potential is not a fuel. It’s a debt you can never pay, because it grows faster than you can earn against it. The better you do, the higher you imagine you could’ve done, so success quietly raises the ceiling instead of letting you touch it. People who run on raw potential never arrive. There’s no coordinate where the gap closes.

What actually helps is replacing potential with a bet. Potential is “I could be anything,” which is infinite and therefore crushing. A bet is “I am spending the next eighteen months on this specific thing because I think it’s the highest-leverage move available to me.” A bet is finite. It can be right or wrong. It can be done. When you’re inside a real bet, the job-versus-potential noise goes quiet, because you’re no longer measuring against an imaginary ceiling — you’re measuring against whether the bet is paying off. That’s a question reality can answer. The ceiling never could.

This is why the most anxious people I know are the most talented ones with no current bet. All that horsepower and nothing specific to push, so it turns inward and grinds on the self. Idle ambition is the worst feeling in the world. Not because you’re lazy — because the engine is running with the clutch in.

Franklin didn’t agonize over his potential. He picked thirteen virtues, made a chart, and worked the chart. He converted an infinite, paralyzing question — how do I become good — into a finite, boring, trackable one. That’s the move. Take the cloud labeled “my potential” and condense it into one bet with a deadline.

So when the guilt comes — and it comes in both states, job and no job — I’ve learned not to argue with it. The guilt is just telling me I don’t currently have a bet I believe in. The answer is never to feel less guilty. It’s to go place the bet.
