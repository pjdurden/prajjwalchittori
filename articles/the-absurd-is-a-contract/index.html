The Absurd Is a Contract

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2026

People think the absurd, in Camus, is a property of the universe — that the world is absurd. It isn’t, quite. Camus is careful here, and the carefulness is the whole insight. The absurd is not in the world and not in you. It’s in the relationship. It’s what arises in the gap between a human being who demands meaning and a universe that stays silent. Take away either party and the absurd vanishes. A universe with no one asking is just a universe. A person with answers feels no absurdity. The absurd is the confrontation. It lives in the hyphen.

This sounds abstract until you realize it’s a contract with two signatures, and you control one of them.

The silent universe is not negotiable. The world owes you no meaning, no fairness, no guarantee that effort pays or that the good thing wins. That signature is fixed; you can’t make the cosmos answer. But the demand — the asking, the insisting on meaning — that’s yours. And here’s where most people make the move Camus spends his whole essay arguing against. Faced with the gap, they try to close it from the wrong side: they invent an answer. They decide the universe does speak — through a system, an ideology, a guaranteed future, a story where it all works out. Camus calls this philosophical suicide. You’ve resolved the absurd by lying about one of the parties.

The other escape is literal despair — deciding that because the universe is silent, nothing is worth doing. Camus rejects this just as hard, and for a sharper reason: it’s a non sequitur. The silence of the universe says nothing about the worth of your acts. You smuggled that in. Meaninglessness-out-there does not imply worthlessness-in-here. Those are different ledgers, and collapsing them is just sloppy thinking dressed up as profundity.

So Camus refuses both exits and holds the gap open on purpose. He keeps the demand and he keeps the honesty about the silence, and he lives in the tension between them without resolving it. That tension, held without flinching, is what he means by revolt. Not anger — lucidity. The clear-eyed insistence on creating value in a universe you know won’t underwrite it.

For a builder this is the whole operating system, and it’s bracingly practical. You will not get a guarantee. The market is the silent universe — it owes your effort nothing, the good product can lose, the deserving thing can die in obscurity, and no amount of merit forces a signature from the other side of the contract. You can lie about this (it’ll definitely work, the universe rewards hard work, I’m owed an outcome) and get blindsided. You can despair about it (nothing’s worth building if it isn’t guaranteed) and never start. Or you can sign your half of the contract with full knowledge that the other half stays blank, and build anyway — not because you’re promised a return, but because the building is your assertion of value against the silence, and the silence can’t take that from you.

That’s the Sisyphus position translated into work. The rock isn’t guaranteed to stay up; in fact it’s guaranteed not to. You push it anyway, eyes open, no illusions, and the pushing is the meaning because you put it there — into a gap that was never going to fill itself.

Don’t wait for the universe to sign. It won’t. Sign your half and ship.
