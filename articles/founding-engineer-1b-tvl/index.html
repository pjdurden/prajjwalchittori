What I Learned Taking a Liquid-Staking Protocol Past $1B TVL as a Founding Engineer

Prajjwal Chittori · July 2022

I joined Stader Labs as an intern in my final year at Delhi Technological University, and somewhere between writing my first CosmWasm contract and watching the dashboards tick past a billion dollars in staked assets, I became a founding engineer on a ~7-person team building multi-chain liquid staking. This is what that taught me — the parts that generalize beyond crypto.

1. “Founding engineer” means you own the seams, not the features

On a small team there is no one whose job is “the boring glue.” The validator doesn’t talk to the dashboard, the on-chain state doesn’t match the off-chain cache, the new chain integration breaks an assumption three modules deep — and all of that is yours. The most valuable thing I did wasn’t a single clever contract; it was owning the reconciliation between systems that were each individually correct but collectively drifting.

If you want to grow fast early in your career, volunteer for the seams. They’re where you learn how a system actually holds together.

2. At scale, money bugs are not like normal bugs

A web app that drops a request shows a spinner. A staking contract that mishandles a rounding boundary silently misallocates real value, and you find out from users, not tests. Crossing $1B in TVL changed how I write code permanently:

  • Invariants over assertions. I started thinking in terms of properties that must hold across every state transition (total staked = sum of positions, always), not just “does this function return the right number.”
  • Reconciliation is a feature. Build the job that re-derives state from first principles and screams when it disagrees with your stored state. It will catch things your unit tests never imagined.
  • The blast radius defines the review bar. Code whose worst case is “a chart looks wrong” and code whose worst case is “funds are misallocated” should not get the same scrutiny, even if they’re in the same PR.

3. Gas is a product constraint, not an afterthought

A lot of my time went into gas-fee analysis — benchmarking storage patterns and swap costs on Terra/CosmWasm so contract execution stayed economical at scale. The lesson that stuck: on-chain, your data model is your cost model. How you lay out storage is a user-facing decision because someone pays for every byte and every read. Engineers coming from web2 underrate this for months. Internalize it on day one: profile gas the way you’d profile a hot loop.

4. Multi-chain forces honesty about your abstractions

Supporting liquidity pools across multiple chains meant constantly confronting which of my “general” abstractions were actually just the first chain’s quirks wearing a trenchcoat. Every new chain integration is a test of whether your core logic is genuinely chain-agnostic or secretly coupled. Cross-chain architecture and on-chain/off-chain sync pipelines are unglamorous, but they’re where the real design happens.

5. The validator-analytics platform taught me more than the contracts

I built a system monitoring 1M+ nodes (React, MongoDB, Ethereum APIs) with APR dashboards. Watching real validator behavior at that scale is the fastest way to understand what your protocol actually incentivizes versus what you intended. The on-chain logic defines the rules; the analytics tell you the game people are really playing. If you build DeFi, instrument the behavior before you tune the mechanism.

Why this still shapes how I work

Today I work at the intersection of crypto and traditional payments — systems where on-chain state has to reconcile with real money movement to the cent. That’s the same seam I learned to love at Stader, just with a card network on the other side. The billion-dollar lesson wasn’t any one technique. It was a posture: assume your two sources of truth will diverge, and make the divergence loud.
