Fiserv — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · September 2020

Nobody has ever had a Fiserv account, and that’s the whole point. Fiserv is the company your bank runs on while pretending it doesn’t. When you log into a regional bank’s app, when a credit union posts a transaction overnight in a batch, when a debit card clears through a network you’ve never heard of — there’s a real chance the machinery underneath is Fiserv’s. They built one of the most important companies in finance by being completely invisible to the people whose money they move.

The insight, which is the opposite of glamorous and exactly right for its era: most banks should not build their own technology. There are thousands of small and mid-sized banks and credit unions in the US, and each one needs a core ledger, account processing, card issuing, bill pay, the whole stack — and none of them can afford to build or maintain it. Fiserv understood that you could be the shared back office for an entire fragmented industry. Sell the picks and shovels to every prospector at once. The fragmentation that looks like a weakness of American banking is precisely what created Fiserv’s market.

And the moat they got right is the ugliest, strongest kind: switching cost. A bank’s core system is load-bearing in the most literal sense — ripping it out is a multi-year, bet-the-institution project that boards are terrified of. So the contract renews, and renews, and renews. Fiserv didn’t build a moat. It became the foundation, and you don’t replace your foundation to save on rent.

Where I’m directionally critical: a company whose entire advantage is being the irreplaceable incumbent has very little incentive to make the future arrive faster. The core-banking model is built on batch processing, decades-old assumptions, and the comfortable knowledge that the customer can’t leave. That’s a great position and a quiet danger. The modern banking-as-a-service and real-time-everything movement is, in part, a rebellion against exactly this — younger infrastructure that’s API-first and real-time-native, built for people who find batch processing offensive. Fiserv’s acquisition of a modern acquiring business was a recognition that the invisible-back-office franchise alone won’t carry it forward. The challenge for the picks-and-shovels king is that someone is forging better shovels, and your customers are slow to switch right up until the moment they aren’t.

Favorite & worst CEO

Favorite: Jeffery Yabuki. He ran Fiserv through a long stretch of disciplined focus, sharpened what the company was, and set up the move into modern payments. His era is the one where Fiserv felt like it had a clear operating thesis rather than just inertia, and the big acquiring acquisition that reshaped it grew out of that direction.

Least connected to: I’ll frame this as an era rather than a person — the deep legacy years where Fiserv’s identity was, by design, “the dependable invisible incumbent.” Admirable engineering reliability, genuinely important plumbing. But it’s a vision of being indispensable rather than being ahead, and indispensability is the kind of moat I respect without being inspired by.
