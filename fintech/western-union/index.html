Western Union — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · February 2023

Western Union understood one thing so deeply that they’re still alive 175 years later despite getting almost everything else wrong: people don’t send money, they send obligation. The maid in Dubai wiring home to Kerala, the construction worker in Qatar funding a sister’s wedding — that’s not a financial transaction, it’s love with a receipt. And love does not comparison-shop as ruthlessly as a spreadsheet predicts. That single human truth has subsidized one of the most criticized fee structures in finance for decades.

The insight they got right, and got right a century before fintech existed: the last mile is the whole business. Anyone can move a number between two cities. The hard part — the part nobody wanted to build — is the cash agent in a village with no banking infrastructure, who hands physical money to a person with no bank account, no smartphone, sometimes no formal ID. Western Union built that physical mesh across every corridor on earth, in the places banks refused to go because the unit economics looked insane. That network of half a million agents is the actual product. The wire is just plumbing.

Where they got it wrong, directionally and badly: they confused the moat with the price. For years their thesis was “the corridor belongs to us, so the margin belongs to us,” and they read their pricing power as permanent. It wasn’t. It was just a head start. When smartphones reached the sender and the agent network stopped being the only way to reach the receiver, every assumption inverted. The digital challengers didn’t beat Western Union by being cheaper — they beat them by making the village agent optional. Western Union’s strength became dead weight the moment the receiver had a phone.

The deeper lesson: an incumbent’s greatest asset is the one it can’t bring itself to cannibalize. Western Union’s agents were a relationship business, and you cannot disrupt yourself when your own distribution would riot.

Favorite & worst CEO

Multiple CEOs across a long arc. Favorite: Hikmet Ersek. He inherited a cash empire and actually said the quiet part — that digital was coming for the core — and pushed the company toward online and mobile when the legacy organization had every incentive to deny it. I connect with the executive who tells his own franchise an uncomfortable truth.

Least connected to: I’ll frame it as an era rather than a person — the long stretch where leadership treated the corridor monopoly as a birthright and let pricing complacency stand in for product vision. Devin McGranahan’s more recent turn toward cost discipline and digital reinvention is the harder, less glamorous work of a company finally racing the clock it ignored too long.
