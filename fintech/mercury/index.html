Mercury — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · October 2020

Mercury understood something almost embarrassingly simple: founders hate their bank. Not in the abstract — viscerally, in the way you hate a thing you’re forced to use that was clearly designed by someone who has never once been you. Legacy business banking is branch hours, fax-coded forms, a dashboard from 2004, and a relationship manager who calls you “valued customer.” Mercury looked at that and said, what if a bank for startups was just good software made by people who’d actually started startups. That’s the whole pitch. It shouldn’t be enough to build a company on. It was.

What they understood that the neobank crowd didn’t: don’t chase consumers, chase founders. The consumer neobank wars were a bloodbath of CAC and razor-thin margins fighting over people who’d switch for a $5 bonus. Mercury picked the narrow, high-value wedge — tech startups, the ones with real deposits, fast growth, and a desperate need for something that doesn’t suck — and served them so well they became the default before a founder even thought to shop. Owning a small, valuable, loyal segment completely beats owning a sliver of everyone. Startups are the best customers in the world: they grow, they refer, and they don’t haggle over fees when the product respects their time.

Where they got it directionally right: a startup’s bank account is the root of its financial graph. Get there first — usually before the company even has revenue — and you’re positioned to layer on everything that follows: corporate cards, treasury, bill pay, and the back-office software a growing company eventually needs. Mercury grasped that “bank account” is the beachhead, not the business, and that the durable play is becoming the financial operating layer a company never has reason to leave. Same insight Brex and Ramp had from the card side; Mercury came at it from the deposit side, which is arguably the stickier root.

Where they’re exposed, directionally: Mercury doesn’t hold a bank charter — they sit on partner banks via banking-as-a-service. That kept them fast and capital-light, and it’s the right call for getting to market. But it means a chunk of their fate is tied to partner-bank relationships and the regulatory weather around BaaS, which has gotten stormier as supervisors scrutinize the fintech-bank middle layer harder. The deeper tension is that their whole edge is software-and-trust, and trust in a place you keep your money is fragile in a way a card isn’t. They’ve navigated it well. But “best UX on someone else’s charter” is a position you have to keep earning, because the moat is taste and reliability, and taste is copyable while reliability is unforgiving.

Favorite & worst CEO

Founder-led, so this is on its leadership. Immad Akhund is one of the sharpest founder-market-fit reads in fintech: a repeat founder who built and sold before, who knew exactly what a startup’s banking pain felt like because he’d lived it, and who chose the unglamorous wedge — make the bank not suck — over chasing a flashier consumer story. The discipline to stay narrow, to serve founders obsessively rather than spray for total addressable market, is the part I most respect; restraint is rarer than ambition. His instinct that the account is the root of the financial graph, not the end of it, is exactly right. My one critique is the structural one — building extraordinary trust and product on partner-bank rails means part of your destiny is rented, and the strongest version of Mercury keeps reducing that dependency as it scales. But as a study in picking the right customer and refusing to dilute, his Mercury is close to a model case.
