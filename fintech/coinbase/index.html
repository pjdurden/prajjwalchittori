Coinbase — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · February 2022

Coinbase bet that the killer feature in crypto was a thing crypto people hated: a front door you couldn’t lose. The whole ethos was “not your keys, not your coins,” self-custody, sovereignty, burn the banks. And Coinbase showed up and said, fine, but most people want a buy button and a password reset. That was heresy. It was also correct.

What they understood that the cypherpunks didn’t: regulatory trust is a product. Not a tax, not a compromise — a feature you sell. Every other early exchange optimized for “what can we get away with,” and Coinbase optimized for “what will still be standing when the subpoena arrives.” In a casino where every competitor blew up — Mt. Gox, then a long parade of them, then FTX — being boring and audited turned out to be the most aggressive long-term position on the board. Survival is a strategy. They were the house that paid out.

Where they got it directionally right: the exchange is the on-ramp, but the on-ramp is not the business. The business is becoming the regulated rails layer for everything that happens after the on-ramp — custody for institutions, USDC plumbing, staking, and Base, which is the real tell. Base is Coinbase admitting that the long game isn’t trading fees, it’s owning a settlement layer that other people build on, then quietly taking sequencer economics off the top. That’s a Visa-shaped insight dressed as an L2. I respect it deeply.

Where they’re exposed, directionally: Coinbase’s identity is fused to American legitimacy, and that’s a double-edged moat. It made them the institutional default. It also means their roadmap is partly written by whoever runs the SEC that year. A company whose core differentiator is “we follow the rules” is structurally dependent on the rules being knowable. For most of their history they weren’t. They turned that ambiguity into a courtroom brand — fighting in public, being the adult — and it worked. But it’s a strange thing to build a moat out of someone else’s indecision.

The deepest thing they got right: crypto’s adoption curve runs through people who will never read a whitepaper. Coinbase built for them and let everyone else feel superior about it.

Favorite & worst CEO

One founder-CEO here, so this is on its leadership. Brian Armstrong is the most underrated operator in crypto, precisely because he’s not entertaining. No theatrics, no airdrop populism, just a flat conviction that the regulated path is the only path that compounds. His “mission-focused, no politics at work” memo cost him talent and earned him a culture that survived 2022 intact — and in this industry, surviving 2022 intact is the entire scoreboard. The Base launch is the clearest sign he understands the arc: stop renting legitimacy to traders, start owning the layer they settle on. My one critique is the inverse of the praise — the same legalism that kept them alive can read as waiting for permission. The best version of Armstrong’s Coinbase stops asking and just becomes the rail. They’re closer to that than people think.
