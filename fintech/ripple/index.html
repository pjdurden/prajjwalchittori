Ripple — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · September 2024

Ripple is the most interesting wrong-shaped right bet in crypto. The thesis was beautiful and early: cross-border payments are broken, correspondent banking is a chain of pre-funded accounts and three-day delays, and a fast ledger with a bridge asset could collapse all of it. That’s correct. SWIFT moves messages, not money — the actual settlement crawls through nostro/vostro accounts that banks pre-stuff with trapped capital. Ripple looked at that and said: what if the float could be liquid, on-demand, in seconds. As a problem statement, it’s one of the best in the entire industry.

What they understood that the Bitcoin crowd didn’t: banks aren’t the enemy, they’re the customer. While everyone else was writing manifestos about abolishing intermediaries, Ripple went to the intermediaries and said, let me make your worst cost center disappear. Selling infrastructure to incumbents instead of trying to replace them is, frankly, the more grown-up business — it’s the lane I’d bet on too. The vision of XRP as a neutral bridge asset that lets you avoid pre-funding currency corridors is genuinely elegant payments engineering.

Where it went directionally sideways: the asset and the company got fused in a way that created a decade of ambiguity about what XRP even is. Is it a bridge asset, a speculation, a thing the company sells to fund itself? The long US legal fight over XRP’s status froze the most important market — American institutions — at exactly the moment the payments thesis needed enterprise trust to compound. They eventually got a meaningful court outcome distinguishing programmatic from institutional sales, but the years lost to that fog were years the on-demand-liquidity vision couldn’t fully ship at home. The technology was ready before the legitimacy was, and in payments, legitimacy is the product.

The other directional miss: stablecoins ate the bridge-asset thesis from the side. The cleanest way to avoid pre-funding turned out to be “just send a tokenized dollar,” and USDC/USDT delivered that with less conceptual baggage than a volatile bridge token. Ripple saw the right problem half a decade early and arguably watched a simpler answer walk through the door it built. That they’ve now leaned into their own stablecoin and custody tells me they know it.

Favorite & worst CEO

Two figures worth naming. Chris Larsen, co-founder and chairman, is the one whose vision I connect with most — a serial fintech founder (he’d already built peer-to-peer lending before Ripple) who saw the trapped-capital problem in correspondent banking with unusual clarity. That founding insight is the durable part of Ripple. Brad Garlinghouse, as CEO, ran the harder and less romantic job: enterprise sales, a multi-year legal siege, and keeping the company solvent and shipping through a regulatory winter most companies wouldn’t survive. I respect the resilience enormously, but the era I connect with least is precisely the one where the company’s energy went into being right in court rather than being early in the market — necessary, even admirable, but it’s litigation as strategy, and the original payments thesis deserved to be defended on rails, not in filings. The best Ripple is the one that out-ships the stablecoin incumbents on actual corridors. The vision was always good enough to.
