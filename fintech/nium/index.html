Nium — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · January 2025

Nium made a bet that most people in fintech are too consumer-brained to take seriously: the winner in cross-border won’t be the app the end user touches — it’ll be the invisible network the apps run on. Nium doesn’t want your loyalty. Nium wants to be the rails that the company you’re loyal to is secretly using. It’s a B2B2X thesis, and B2B2X is unglamorous, and unglamorous is exactly where durable infrastructure businesses get built.

The insight they understood that others didn’t: the world doesn’t need another remittance brand, it needs a single connection that reaches everywhere. Every bank, fintech, and platform that wants to pay out globally faces the same nightmare — integrate separately with each country’s payout method, hold licenses you don’t have, manage FX and compliance in markets you’ve never operated in. Nium’s pitch was: connect to us once, and reach the planet. One API, real-time payouts into a huge share of the world’s bank accounts and wallets. They turned “the hard part of global money” into a vendor relationship. That’s a real product.

What they got right directionally: own the network of last-mile payout connections, because that’s the part that compounds. Anyone can build a nice dashboard; almost no one will do the multi-year grind of plugging into local clearing systems across dozens of countries and keeping the licenses current. The payout reach is the asset that gets more valuable with every corridor added and is correspondingly miserable to replicate.

Where they’re directionally exposed: Nium sits in a knife fight with Airwallex, Wise Platform, Thunes, and the card networks all reaching for the same “global money infrastructure” crown — and the buyers are sophisticated enterprises who will multi-home across providers and squeeze the margin without mercy. Being infrastructure means your customers are smart, demanding, and allergic to lock-in. The arc bends toward whoever has the broadest real-time reach at the lowest cost with the cleanest compliance record — a race that rewards relentless, boring excellence over any clever narrative.

Favorite & worst CEO

On its leadership: Prajit Nanu, co-founder and CEO. I have a soft spot for the origin — the story goes that he couldn’t easily send money for a friend’s wedding and decided the entire B2B plumbing was the real thing worth fixing, not the consumer app. That instinct — to go down a layer, to the rails, away from the shiny front-end where everyone else is fighting — is the correct instinct, and it’s the one I’d make myself. The founder risk is the perennial infrastructure-founder trap: a global rails business demands ruthless focus and operational discipline across many markets at once, and ambition that outpaces execution is how promising networks stall. The vision is right. Infrastructure punishes anything less than relentless on the follow-through.
