Galileo — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2020

Galileo is the company you’ve used a hundred times and never heard of, which is exactly the company I find most interesting. Behind a startling number of the cards and accounts you think of as “neobanks,” there’s an issuer-processor doing the actual work — authorizing the transaction, posting it to a ledger, talking to the card networks, handling the unglamorous machinery between a swipe and a balance. For a long stretch of the fintech boom, that company was disproportionately Galileo. They were the engine in cars with someone else’s logo on the hood.

What they understood that the app-builders didn’t: the hard part of a neobank isn’t the app, it’s the processor. Anyone can ship a slick mobile UI over a weekend — I’ve built that part. What you can’t build over a weekend is a real-time, ledger-correct, network-certified, compliance-aware system that authorizes payments without losing money or breaking rules at scale. Galileo’s bet was that hundreds of fintechs would want to be a bank-shaped app without building that engine themselves, and that selling the engine — open APIs for issuing cards and running accounts — was a better business than competing in the app layer. Picks and shovels during a gold rush. It’s the lane I respect most because it’s the one that’s actually hard.

Where they were directionally right: they treated “issue a card programmatically” as a developer primitive years before that was obvious. They helped turn launching a card program from a multi-year bank negotiation into an integration, and that abstraction is a real piece of the reason the 2010s fintech explosion happened at all. Lowering the cost of starting a fintech to an API call is the kind of infrastructure move that quietly enables an entire era. The whole neobank wave partly stands on processors like Galileo making the boring part buyable.

Where the arc gets complicated: in 2020 Galileo was acquired by SoFi, and that’s the directional tension I keep turning over. A neutral, arms-dealer processor is most valuable precisely because it’s neutral — fintechs trust it because it isn’t trying to become them. Once it sits inside SoFi, a company that is itself a consumer fintech, every customer has to ask whether they’re powering their infrastructure on a competitor’s stack. SoFi gets a strategic engine and a B2B revenue line; Galileo gets capital and distribution. But the independence that made the arms dealer trustworthy is exactly the thing an acquisition complicates. Infrastructure wants to be Switzerland. Owning it makes you a country.

Favorite & worst CEO

Two eras here. Galileo was founded by Clay Wilkes, and his is the vision I connect with most — he saw, early and unfashionably, that the durable position in payments wasn’t the consumer brand but the processing engine underneath it, and he built a deep, hard, infrastructure business at a time when everyone else wanted to be the shiny front end. Building the unsexy primitive that others depend on is, to me, the highest form of fintech craft. The era I connect with least, as a matter of vision rather than execution, is the post-2020 chapter under SoFi’s umbrella (Anthony Noto as SoFi’s CEO) — not because it’s badly run, but because folding a neutral processor into a consumer-fintech parent trades the thing that made Galileo special, its Switzerland status, for scale and strategic fit. A sound corporate decision. Just the opposite of the founding instinct that made it matter.
