Venmo — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · December 2020

Venmo turned money into a social object, and that is either the best or the worst idea in the history of consumer finance — I still can’t decide. Before Venmo, a payment was private by default. After Venmo, paying your friend rent had a public feed with emojis. They took the most awkward conversation between two people — who owes whom — and made it a status update. That’s not a payments feature. That’s a behavioral hack on shame.

The insight the founders understood that the banks never could: money between friends is not a transaction, it’s an event in a relationship. Splitting a dinner check, covering a concert ticket, paying back the friend who spotted you — these are social moments first and ledger entries second. Every bank built the ledger and ignored the social. Venmo built the social and let the ledger ride along for free. The feed wasn’t a gimmick. It was the distribution engine — you onboarded because your roommate paid you and you had to see what the 🍕 meant.

What they got right directionally: friction is the enemy of P2P, and the network only has value once everyone you’d ever pay is already on it. They won the cold-start by being the default among college kids, and college kids graduate into the entire economy carrying the habit. That’s the cleanest demographic land-grab in fintech. Own the 19-year-olds and wait.

Where they got it wrong directionally: Venmo spent a decade being a verb without being a business. They proved you could own the social layer of money and then struggled to charge for it without breaking the thing that made it spread. The user fell in love with “free and social.” Monetizing that — cards, merchant checkout, crypto, instant transfer fees — always risks feeling like a betrayal of the original gift. The arc bends toward a product that everyone uses and no one quite lets you charge for.

Favorite & worst CEO

Multiple eras, so: favorite is the founding vision of Andrew Kortina and Iqram Magdon-Ismail — the original insight that a payment could be a social act was genuinely new, and almost everything good about Venmo descends from it. They saw the feed before anyone knew a feed belonged on money.

Least connected to: the PayPal stewardship era after the Braintree acquisition. Not for any failing of character — purely vision. Under PayPal, Venmo became a monetization asset to be optimized inside a portfolio, and the founders’ wild “money is social” spark got managed into a revenue line. Competent custody of someone else’s idea is the era I feel the least thesis in.
