Ramp — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · December 2022

Ramp did the one thing no other corporate card company would say out loud: they built a product whose explicit goal is to make you spend less. Every card business on Earth, since the dawn of plastic, makes more money the more you swipe. Interchange rewards volume. So the entire industry is structurally incentivized to nudge you toward spending. Ramp showed up and said our job is to find the duplicate SaaS subscription, kill the unused seat, flag the price you overpaid, and shrink your bill. That’s not a feature. That’s pointing the gun the other way.

What they understood that everyone else missed: the buyer of a corporate card isn’t the spender, it’s the finance team — and the finance team’s actual job is to control money, not move it. By aligning the product with the customer’s real incentive (spend less, see everything, close the books faster) instead of the vendor’s incentive (swipe more), Ramp built trust that pure interchange players structurally can’t. It’s the rare case where the honest position is also the winning position. You earn the CFO’s loyalty by saving them money, and loyalty is stickier than rewards points.

Where they were directionally right: finance back-office is a workflow problem wearing a payments costume. The card is the easy part. The hard, valuable part is everything around it — approvals, receipts, accounting sync, bill pay, procurement, the soul-crushing month-end close. Ramp treated the corporate card as the data-collection layer for an automation platform, and then attacked the workflows with software and, increasingly, AI agents that do the reconciliation a human used to. That’s the right altitude. Whoever automates the finance team’s tedious 80% owns the relationship, and the card just funds it.

Where they’re exposed, directionally: the “save you money” thesis and the interchange revenue model live in permanent tension. Ramp makes money on spend volume while marketing itself on spend reduction. So far they’ve squared it by growing into bigger customers faster than they shrink any single one’s bill — net more volume across more companies even as each gets leaner. That works while they’re winning logos. It gets philosophically interesting if growth ever slows, because then the incentive to actually reduce spend collides with the incentive to keep volume up. And the Brex convergence is real — same rails, same category, same feature race. Ramp’s edge has to stay being the most ruthless about efficiency, because that’s the one position that can’t be copied without copying the honesty.

Favorite & worst CEO

Founder-led, so this is on its leadership. Eric Glyman built Ramp around a genuinely contrarian act of product courage: an interchange-funded business that openly tries to reduce the very spend it earns on. Most founders would never deliberately point their product against their own revenue line; Glyman made that contradiction the brand, and trusted that customer trust would compound faster than short-term swipe revenue. That’s a real thesis, not a tagline. The AI-native push — treating the finance back office as a set of tasks software agents should just do — is the clearest sign he understands where the value is migrating: from the card to the automation. My one critique is the inherent one in the model: a company whose pitch is efficiency has to keep proving the efficiency is real and not just a customer-acquisition story, especially as it scales into bigger, messier enterprises. The strongest version of Glyman’s Ramp is the autonomous finance team — and that’s a vision worth betting on.
