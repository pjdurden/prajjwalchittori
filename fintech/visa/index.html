Visa — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · November 2023

Visa is not a payments company. That’s the thing almost everyone gets wrong, including people who work there. Visa is a set of rules. It is a court that no one ever has to walk into, a language that banks who hate each other agree to speak, a trust protocol that happens to clear money. The cards are a costume. The real product is consensus.

Dee Hock understood this before anyone had the vocabulary. He built a network owned by its competitors, which should have been impossible, and the reason it worked is that he made the network bigger than any member could be alone. No one defects from a thing that only has value because no one defects. That’s not finance. That’s game theory wearing a suit.

What Visa got right, directionally, is that the winning move in payments is never the transaction. It’s the dispute. Anyone can move a number from one ledger to another — I’ve written that code, it’s a weekend. What’s hard is what happens when the merchant lies, or the card was stolen, or the goods never shipped. Visa built the machinery for “what if it goes wrong” and then sold the happy path for free. Chargebacks are the moat. The fraud liability shift is the moat. The four-party model exists so that no single party can hold the network hostage, and that constraint is the whole genius.

Where they’re directionally exposed: Visa’s instinct is to absorb every new rail rather than be threatened by it. Visa Direct, account-to-account, tokenization, crypto settlement pilots — they extend the tentacles outward so that whatever wins, Visa is the toll booth on the on-ramp. Smart, defensively. But it’s the posture of an incumbent who has decided the future is a variation of the present. On-chain settlement is not a variation. When the ledger itself enforces the rules, you don’t need a court. That’s the one bet Visa structurally cannot make, because the court IS the company.

They know this. That’s why they buy.

Favorite & worst CEO

Favorite: Dee Hock — though “CEO” undersells him. He invented the organizational form. Most executives optimize a machine someone else built; he built a machine whose shape had no precedent. Everything Visa is today is a footnote to his founding insight that you can align enemies if the network rewards cooperation more than defection.

Least connected to: Charlie Scharf’s era. Competent, disciplined, a clean operator — but his Visa was a stewardship Visa, run like a toll road to be maintained rather than a frontier to be pushed. I respect the execution. I just don’t feel a thesis in it.
