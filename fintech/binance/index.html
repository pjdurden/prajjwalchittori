Binance — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · April 2023

Binance won by refusing to have an address. While everyone else was negotiating with a single regulator and a single jurisdiction, Binance treated the entire planet as one liquidity pool and treated borders as latency. That was the insight, and it was a brutal one: in a global, permissionless market, the firm with no home wallet beats the firm that has to file paperwork in one country. For years they were everywhere and nowhere, and that wasn’t sloppiness — it was the strategy.

What they understood that Coinbase didn’t: speed of listing IS the product. Coinbase curated; Binance shipped. New token, new pair, new perp, new chain — Binance was first, deepest, and cheapest, and in a market where liquidity begets liquidity, being first to list and deepest on the book is a flywheel that’s almost impossible to attack head-on. They built BNB Chain, Launchpad, the whole vertical stack, so that they weren’t just the exchange — they were the gravity well the entire alt market orbited. At peak they were a majority of global spot volume. That’s not a company, that’s weather.

The directional cost is now public record, and I’ll state it plainly and without piling on. In 2023 Binance reached a settlement with US authorities over AML and sanctions-compliance failures; CZ stepped down as CEO and the company paid a very large penalty. That’s the bill for “no address.” The thing that made them unbeatable — being faster than the rulebook — was the same thing that eventually had to be reconciled. You can outrun jurisdiction for a long time. You cannot outrun it forever, and the genius of the model and its liability were always the same fact viewed from two sides.

Where I think they were directionally right anyway: the future of finance is global-by-default, and someone had to prove the demand was real before the compliant version could exist. Binance ran that experiment at a scale no one else dared. Coinbase gets to be the regulated heir partly because Binance already showed the whole world wanted in. Pioneers take the arrows. Binance took a quiver.

Favorite & worst CEO

Two eras, so: favorite and least-favorite as an opinion about vision, not character.

Favorite vision: Changpeng Zhao, “CZ.” Whatever you make of the ending, the founding thesis — that crypto liquidity is global and the winner is whoever serves it fastest with no home-jurisdiction tax — was the single most correct read of the 2017–2021 market. He built the deepest book on Earth in under four years from a standing start. As pure product-and-market intuition, it’s generational.

Least connected to: the current Richard Teng era — and this is not a knock on Teng, who I think is exactly the right operator for the moment. It’s that his mandate is to convert a borderless pirate ship into a licensed, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction institution. Necessary, mature, correct. But it’s a stewardship thesis, and the thing that made Binance Binance was the opposite of stewardship. I respect the cleanup. I just felt more thesis in the chaos.
