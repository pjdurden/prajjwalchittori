Brex — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · February 2020

Brex started with one sharp, almost rude observation: startups had money but no credit. A two-person company sitting on a $5M seed round still got denied a corporate card, because the underwriting models were built for revenue and personal guarantees, not for a bank balance and a YC batch. Brex looked at that gap and said, we’ll underwrite the bank account, not the founder. Give a startup a real corporate card on day one, no personal guarantee, limits based on cash on hand. Obvious in hindsight. Most great fintech ideas are — they’re a mispricing someone finally noticed.

What they understood that the banks didn’t: the card is a wedge, not the product. The reason to give startups a card with no friction isn’t the interchange — it’s that the card is how you enter a company’s financial nervous system at its birth, before it has a CFO, before it has habits, before it picks a bank. Get there first and you own the spend data, the expense flows, the bill pay, the whole back office. Brex grasped early that the modern company runs on software, so the company card should be the front end of a software suite, not a piece of plastic with a statement.

Where they were directionally right and then had to be brave: they made a hard pivot away from the long tail of SMBs toward larger, venture-backed and enterprise customers. That looked, from the outside, like abandoning their origin story. I read it differently — it was a clear-eyed admission that the “card for everyone” lane was getting crowded and commoditized, and that the durable business is being the financial operating system for serious, scaling companies. Choosing fewer, deeper customers over more, shallower ones is usually the more mature call, even when it costs you the founder myth.

Where they’re exposed, directionally: Brex and Ramp now look more alike than either would like, and the spend-management category they helped invent is brutally competitive — same banking-as-a-service rails, similar interchange model, overlapping feature checklists. When two well-funded companies converge on the same product, the differentiator stops being the product and becomes culture, focus, and who can resist the temptation to do everything. The risk for Brex was always scope creep — card, then cash, then bill pay, then travel, then spend software — and the discipline question is whether the platform feels like one coherent operating system or a pile of acquired adjacencies. The pivot suggests they know. Focus is the moat in a category this crowded.

Favorite & worst CEO

Co-founder-led, so this is on its leadership. Henrique Dubugras and Pedro Franceschi are, to me, the most impressive raw founder talent in the spend-management cohort — two Brazilian teenagers who’d already built and sold a payments company before they could legally rent a car in the US, then attacked corporate cards with the speed of people who simply don’t believe gatekeepers are real. The “underwrite the cash, not the founder” insight is exactly the kind of obvious-in-retrospect mispricing that only outsiders see. The part of their arc I most respect is also the part that took the most nerve: the willingness to pivot away from the SMB origin story toward enterprise, killing their own founding narrative because the data said the durable business was elsewhere. My one critique is the standing one for any founder in a hot, convergent category — the appetite to expand surface area can outrun the discipline to perfect it. The strongest version of their vision is Brex as the single financial OS for the modern company, chosen for depth, not breadth. When they hold that line, they’re excellent.
