Remitly — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · January 2024

Remitly is the company that read Western Union’s business correctly and then built the inverse of it. Where the incumbent’s thesis was “own the cash agent,” Remitly’s thesis was “the immigrant has a smartphone now, so meet them inside their actual life — their language, their corridor, their pay cycle, their fear.” They didn’t try to be a bank or a network. They picked a single human being — the diaspora sender — and refused to build anything that didn’t serve her specifically.

The insight they understood that horizontal fintechs miss: remittance is not one market, it’s a thousand corridors, and each corridor is its own product. The US-to-Philippines flow has nothing in common with the UK-to-Nigeria flow except a logo. Different payout methods, different trust signals, different anxieties. A generalist player treats “international transfer” as one feature. Remitly treated each corridor as its own go-to-market, its own compliance posture, its own emotional contract. That’s slower and harder and it’s exactly why it works.

What they got right directionally: trust is the entire product in remittance, because the sender is wiring money she cannot afford to lose to a family that’s counting on it by a specific day. Remitly leaned into reliability and a delivery promise instead of screaming about the lowest fee — because the sender doesn’t actually optimize on fee, she optimizes on “will it arrive, intact, on time, the way it said it would.” Get that right and the fee debate barely matters.

Where they’re directionally exposed: remittance is a brutal business with no structural moat. Corridors are contestable, FX margins compress every year, and the next entrant always undercuts you to buy the same migrant. Remitly’s defense has to be brand trust and operational excellence, which are real but expensive moats that you re-earn every quarter. The arc bends toward a financial relationship that outlives the single transaction — lending, savings, the whole immigrant financial life — because the send alone is a race to zero.

Favorite & worst CEO

On its leadership: Matt Oppenheimer. Founder-CEO, ex-Barclays, and the rare fintech founder whose origin story is actually load-bearing — he watched the friction of moving money across borders firsthand and built the company as a corrective rather than an arbitrage. I connect with the discipline of staying narrow: a founder who could have chased “global neobank” buzzwords and instead spent a decade obsessing over making one immigrant’s transfer land on time. The conviction that the customer is a specific person, not a TAM slide, is the most honest thing a fintech founder can have. The open question is whether that focus can carry the company past the part where the core product is structurally a commodity.
