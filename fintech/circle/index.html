Circle — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · April 2020

Circle figured out that the most valuable thing in crypto is the most boring number on the screen: 1.00. Everyone else was building rockets — tokens that go up, chains that go fast, yields that defy arithmetic. Circle built a thing whose entire job is to not move. A dollar that’s also software. And it turns out the killer app of crypto was never the volatility. It was the stillness — a dollar you can program, send anywhere, in seconds, without asking a bank for permission.

What they understood that the maximalists didn’t: stablecoins aren’t a crypto feature, they’re a dollar-distribution feature. USDC is the US dollar with an API. I’ve moved value over SWIFT and over USDC, and the difference isn’t incremental — it’s the difference between mailing a letter and sending a packet. Circle’s bet was that the world wants dollars more than it wants any token, and the chain is just the cheapest pipe ever invented for delivering them. That’s correct, and it’s a bigger market than all of speculative crypto combined, because it’s the actual dollar economy choosing better rails.

Where they were directionally right and brave: they chose Tether’s exact opposite. Tether is the offshore, opaque, “trust us” incumbent that won on distribution. Circle chose attestations, US regulation, transparency, and the long slog of being the auditable stablecoin. For years that looked like fighting with one hand tied. But as stablecoins go from gray-market plumbing to regulated money infrastructure, the auditable one is the one that gets to plug into the banking system instead of getting unplugged from it. Slow legitimacy beats fast ambiguity once the regulators actually arrive. They’ve been arriving.

Where they’re exposed: Circle’s economics are someone else’s interest rates. Strip away the crypto-native framing and a huge chunk of the business is earning yield on the reserves backing the float — which means the core engine is rate policy, not product genius. That’s a magnificent business in a high-rate world and a nervous one otherwise. The deeper risk is that “dollar with an API” is a category, not a moat — banks, Coinbase via the USDC relationship, and the whole stablecoin-act cohort all want that lane. Circle’s edge has to be that they were the trusted, regulated default before the category got crowded. Being early to legitimacy is a real moat. It just has to be defended like one.

Favorite & worst CEO

Founder-led, so this is on its leadership. Jeremy Allaire is a rare two-arc founder: he built internet-video infrastructure in the 90s, then did it again for money. The through-line is that he keeps betting on open protocols beating closed products, and on regulation as a moat rather than an enemy — which in crypto, where the prevailing instinct is to flee jurisdiction, is genuinely contrarian conviction. Choosing the transparent, US-regulated path against Tether’s offshore juggernaut was a decade-long patience trade, and patience is the rarest asset in this industry. My one critique is that the same instinct can tip into being so consensus-and-compliance-shaped that the product roadmap waits on legislation. The strongest version of Allaire’s Circle treats USDC less like a regulated token and more like a settlement standard the whole internet defaults to — and acts like it already won.
