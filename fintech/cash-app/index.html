Cash App — What I Think

Prajjwal Chittori · June 2022

Cash App is the most underestimated product in American fintech, and the reason is that the people who underestimate it have never been poor. That’s not a slight. It’s the whole thesis. Cash App grew where the banks weren’t, in the gaps the credit-score economy leaves behind, and it did it by being the first finance product in a long time that didn’t make you feel surveilled for using it.

The insight nobody at the legacy banks could afford to have: there is enormous money in the customers everyone else priced out. Overdraft America. Cash-tip America. The kid who got paid in Venmo and never opened a checking account. The banks looked at that population and saw risk and low balances. Cash App looked at it and saw a distribution channel with a viral coefficient. The green app spread the way memes spread, through rap lyrics and $CashTags and the simple fact that it just worked on a cheap Android phone.

What they got right directionally: bundle. Most fintechs do one thing — payments, or investing, or banking. Cash App quietly assembled the whole stack — P2P, debit card, direct deposit, stock fractions, Bitcoin, taxes — inside one icon, for a demographic that was never going to walk into a brokerage. The Bitcoin bet in particular was contrarian and early, and it gave Cash App a revenue engine plus a cultural identity at the same time. They understood that for their user, Bitcoin wasn’t an asset class. It was the first investment that didn’t require permission.

Where they’re directionally exposed: every layer of the bundle is someone’s full company, and those companies are coming for the bundle one tab at a time. And the same low-friction onboarding that made it spread made it a fraud magnet — when a network is built for trust between strangers, the strangers who don’t deserve trust find it fastest. Growth and fraud drink from the same tap. That tension never resolves. You just manage it forever.

Favorite & worst CEO

On its leadership: Cash App lives inside Block, under Jack Dorsey. I connect with the founder-level conviction that Bitcoin belongs in the hands of the un-banked rather than the over-served, and the discipline to ship it years before it was comfortable. The Block-era weakness is the opposite of vision — it’s focus. A company that wants to be Square and Cash App and Tidal and a Bitcoin mining operation is a company betting that one founder’s curiosity scales to four mandates. Vision was never Dorsey’s problem. Saying no was.
